Module 14 · Loss and Grief

Grief Therapy Program

Silence in the room sometimes says more than words. Grief sessions are the ones where I move with the slowest, most careful steps — here, there's no rushing, no fixing, no saying "it'll pass," just being present. Below is a sensitive, staged program for working with loss in children and adults. Every family arrives here at their own pace, and I adjust to that.

1

Two faces of grief: The Dual Process Model

Clinical definition: Per Stroebe and Schut's Dual Process Model, after a loss a person constantly oscillates between two different processes — loss-oriented (confronting the sadness, memory, pain directly) and restoration-oriented (adapting to daily life, new roles). This "back-and-forth" movement isn't pathological, it's the very nature of healthy grieving. If symptoms persist beyond 12 months (6 months in children), significantly impairing functioning, a "Prolonged Grief Disorder" diagnosis per the DSM-5-TR can be considered.
In plain terms: Grief isn't a straight line. It's more like a wave. One day you can laugh, the next a heavy sadness comes. Both are normal. The "5 stages" model is familiar to many, but in real life people don't move through these stages in order — sometimes they go back and forth several times in the same day.

Leyla told me: "Sometimes I laugh, then I feel guilty." My answer was simple — laughing doesn't mean forgetting. It's just the restoration-oriented side switching on for a moment. Both are you.

2

A different face by age

👶 Children

  • Reenacting the loss in play
  • "Will they come back?" questions
  • Regression, sleep problems

🧑 Teens

  • Withdrawal or risky behavior
  • A tendency to hide emotions

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Guilt, "if only" thoughts
  • A decline in daily functioning

Children experience their grief "drop by drop" — they can cry for five minutes, then go play with a toy. Parents sometimes see this as "indifference." It isn't. A child's brain can't digest the pain all at once, it takes it in small doses.

3

Practical technique: The Memory Box

This is one of my favorite techniques, because it doesn't require words. I ask the client (especially a child) to gather small items connected to the person they lost — a photo, a letter, a small gift — and place them in a box. The box stays open, it can be opened at any time.

The goal isn't to "forget" the loss, it's the opposite. Per the theory of continuing bonds, completely cutting the connection with the lost person isn't healthy — continuing the connection in a new form is healthy. The box is a symbol of this new form — the person is gone, but the memory stays, changing shape. Some clients add a new item to the box even years later — this shows the process isn't over, its form has just changed.

Attention: Some families initially resist this technique — they think "seeing the items will cause pain." In my early years, I myself hesitated to suggest this in one family, then I saw that they wanted the box themselves, they just didn't know who would start.
4

A multidisciplinary note

🧠 Psychological perspective

Guilt ("if only I'd acted differently") is the most common, but least spoken, feeling in the grief process.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

Fluctuation in the school performance of a child who has experienced a loss is normal — coordination with the teacher helps create a pressure-free environment.

✋ Occupational therapist perspective

Body fatigue and loss of appetite during grief are real physical symptoms — not just "psychological," but a reaction of the whole organism.

5

One question, one answer

Leyla:When will I feel normal again?
Therapist:Maybe the "old" normal will never come back. But a new normal will form — and it can be good in its own place too.
Note: I hear this question from almost every grief client. In my answer I never give a specific timeframe — because it would be a lie. I just show the direction of the process.

7

A few words for the family

🚫 Don't say "time heals everything"

This sentence is well-intentioned, but it's often heard as empty and distant.

👂 Just listen

You don't need to offer a solution — often just being heard is enough.

📅 Don't forget special days

Extra support is needed on dates like anniversaries, birthdays.

Homework: This week, have the client (with family if they want) start preparing a small memory box — without rushing, choosing the items they want.
8

Leyla's story: the empty chair

When a child looks at their father's empty chair and says "he should still be sitting at the table," this is the hardest moment for a therapist — there's no fixing it, no saying "it'll pass," there's only being present.

📋 Presentation

Leyla was 9 years old, she'd suddenly lost her father 4 months earlier. She couldn't focus at school, had trouble falling asleep at night, and sometimes said her father "would still come home." Her mother was worried the child wasn't "accepting" the loss.

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that Leyla's reaction was within the normal spectrum of children's grief — 9-year-olds don't yet fully cognitively grasp the finality of death, this isn't "denial," it's a developmentally appropriate stage. The real work wasn't to "make her accept it," it was to help her express her feelings safely.

Therapist:Leyla, what do you feel when you think about your dad?
Client:Sometimes I miss him a lot, sometimes it's like I don't feel anything at all.
Therapist:Both are completely normal. Feelings are like waves — they come, they go.
Client:Sometimes I talk to him like he can hear.
Therapist:That's a really lovely thing — your bond with your dad is still going, just in a different form. If you want, we can write him a letter, as many as you want.
Client:Will he read that letter?
Therapist:I don't know how, but putting what's in your heart onto paper itself helps a lot.
Core principle: In children's grief, the goal isn't to "forget" the loss, it's to maintain a healthy continuing bond with the deceased.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Leyla I combined the memory box and letter-writing techniques — when young children struggle to put a feeling into words, a concrete ritual often works far more powerfully than words.

12

Signs of complicated grief

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

A normal grief process softens over time; the following signs indicate a shift toward complicated grief or comorbid depression, and require deepening the program:

  • Functional decline 6-12 months after the loss, a trend of worsening instead of improving;
  • Recurring thoughts about death connected to a wish to reunite with the lost person;
  • Complete denial of the loss, or "continuing the relationship" at a behavioral level with the deceased to an extreme degree (keeping the room untouched, speaking to them excessively);
  • Deepening social isolation, a complete cut-off of previous friendship/family ties.
  1. Directly clarify with a question the difference between thoughts related to death as a "wish to reunite" versus "intent to self-harm."
  2. If decline is observed after 6-12 months, refer to a specialized assessment for complicated grief.
  3. If functional isolation deepens, build a gradual social reintegration plan together with the family.
  4. If comorbid depression signs are strong, conduct an additional PHQ-9 screening.

Note: The "normal" course of grief consists of waves, not a line — what raises concern isn't the decline itself, but the decline turning into functional loss.

📌 Module summary

  • Grief isn't a linear process, it's a fluctuation between the loss-oriented and restoration-oriented sides.
  • Continuing bonds — it's not healthy to cut the connection with the lost person, it's healthy to continue it in a new form.
  • The Memory Box technique strengthens this bond symbolically, without requiring words.
  • Children experience their grief "drop by drop" — this isn't indifference, it's a natural mechanism.
