Module 20 · Full ADHD Program

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

People with ADHD often don't lack knowledge of what to do — their difficulty is in applying what they know consistently. That distinction is the most important message in this course's final module. This is a full, multidisciplinary support program for working with ADHD in children, teens, and adults. Every principle we've seen across 20 modules — sensory, cognitive, family-oriented — comes together here.

1

Not a knowledge problem — an execution problem

Sanan once told me: "I know what I'm supposed to do, I just somehow don't do it." I've heard that exact sentence, in that exact form, dozens of times. Sanan knew his class schedule by heart, could even write it down. But actually executing it in the morning — that was a completely different skill, separate from the knowledge itself. Showing a client that distinction often softens years of accumulated self-criticism in a single moment.

Clinical definition: According to Russell Barkley's theory of ADHD, the core problem isn't attention itself, but behavioral inhibition and executive function (working memory, self-regulation, planning, time management). This is explained through the concept of ADHD as "time blindness": it becomes harder to picture the future in the present moment, which leads to tasks being put off.
In plain terms: Imagine there's a "director" in the brain — managing attention, planning, controlling impulses. In ADHD, this "director" works differently — that isn't laziness or "bad parenting," it's a developmental feature of the brain.
2

Clinical case: Sanan's story

Sanan was 14, and even when he "knew" his homework every day, he'd go through 40 minutes of "undefined delay" before actually sitting down at the desk to start. The family called this "lack of willpower." When we explored what was actually happening during that time, we found that Sanan couldn't locate the "starting step" of the task — this wasn't a gap in knowledge, it was a specific weak point in executive function.

Clinical observation: Once I showed this distinction to the family, their whole attitude shifted — it stopped being "go, get to work" and became "let's find the starting step together."

4

A closer look at one session

In session 3 we work on "breaking down the task." The protocol goes like this: first I ask Sanan to pick the hardest task of the week. Then we break it down together into 3 small parts — each one small enough to take no more than 10 minutes. We set a visual timer for each part. As the session ends, I ask Sanan a specific question: "What time tomorrow will you sit down for this task?" — setting a specific time is far more effective than the thought "I'll do it sometime." This small detail — an exact time — often makes the whole difference.

5

Client-therapist dialogue (with Sanan)

Sanan:I know what I should do, I just can't get started.
Therapist:That's not laziness, Sanan. Your brain finds the "start" button in a different spot. Let's find that button together.
Sanan:So there's nothing wrong with me?
Therapist:No. You just work differently. Different wiring needs a different key.
Note: Sanan needed to hear this sentence several times — hearing it once isn't enough to shift years of accumulated self-criticism.

11

Assessment scales

📊

Vanderbilt ADHD Rating Scale

A widely used tool with parent and teacher versions, separately measuring inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.

📊

Conners' Rating Scale (CRS-3)

Provides a detailed profile covering executive function and academic performance.

📊

BRIEF-2 (Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function)

A tool that specifically assesses executive functions like task initiation, planning, and organization.

Note: Sanan's BRIEF-2 result showed particularly high difficulty on the "task initiation" subscale — this played a key role in precisely defining the treatment target.
12

Broad risk screening in the full ADHD program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Unlike a shorter version, the full ADHD program requires broader monitoring of the following comorbid risks:

  • Impulsivity turning into behaviors that create physical danger (disregard for traffic rules, risky games);
  • Ongoing academic/social failure leading to a deep self-esteem crisis;
  • Emotional dysregulation escalating into aggression or self-harm episodes;
  • Increased risk of substance use in adolescence (a well-documented long-term risk factor for ADHD).
  1. If impulsive behavior creating physical danger is found, plan concrete environmental safety measures together with the parent.
  2. If a self-esteem crisis deepens, run an additional mood screening (SDQ/PHQ-9).
  3. If a self-harm episode is found, assess immediately using the Columbia Scale and document the outcome.
  4. Don't hesitate to ask sensitive, non-judgmental questions about signs of substance use in adolescence.

Note: Long-term tracking of ADHD shows that comorbid risks increase with age when early intervention doesn't happen — the value of the full program lies exactly in this broader perspective.

📌 Module summary

  • In ADHD, the core problem is often not knowledge but execution — the ability to consistently apply what's already known.
  • Breaking a task into small parts and setting a specific time reduces procrastination.
  • Multidisciplinary support (psychological, occupational therapy, psychopedagogical, speech-language) completes the full picture.
  • Reducing self-criticism and building the frame "I work differently, I'm not broken" matters for long-term progress.
