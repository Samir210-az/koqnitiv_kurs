Module 13 · Performance Anxiety

Exam Anxiety Program

When Kamilə came to me, she said, "I know all the material for the whole year, but the moment I walk into the exam room, everything gets erased." This is the story of so many students — not a knowledge problem, but an anxiety problem. Below is a complete CBT program for working with exam anxiety in students. This program's strength is in its simplicity — a few concrete skills, taught in the right sequence.

1

Clinical definition: Exam/Performance Anxiety

Clinical definition: Exam anxiety is clinically classified as performance-specific anxiety and is explained by the Yerkes-Dodson law: anxiety has an "inverted-U" shaped relationship with performance — helpful at a moderate level, but at an excessive level it weakens performance by blocking cognitive resources (working memory, attention).
In plain terms: A little anxiety is helpful — it keeps us alert and focused. The problem is when anxiety crosses the peak of the "inverted U" curve and starts hindering performance. The goal isn't to bring anxiety down to zero, it's to keep it at a "working" level.

When I told Kamilə in the first minute, "you know the material, your problem isn't knowledge," I saw relief in her eyes. A lot of students think the feeling of blankness means "not knowing." In reality, this is very often just anxiety temporarily blocking cognitive resources. Understanding this alone already reduces the anxiety a bit, because the thought "I'm stupid" gives way to "this is a temporary state."

2

Clinical view: Symptoms

🧠

Cognitive

"I'll forget everything," distracted attention, a feeling of blankness.

💓

Physical

Heart racing, sweating, stomach ache, insomnia.

📉

Behavioral

Procrastination, a desire to flee the exam room.

3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Younger students

  • Stomach ache, crying, clinging to a parent
  • Difficulty putting the anxiety into words

🧑 Teens and university students

  • Procrastination, the "not studying at all rather than being unprepared" paradox
  • Self-criticism linked to parental/competitive pressure
4

A multidisciplinary view

🧠 Psychological perspective

Catastrophizing ("if I don't pass this exam, my life is over") is the main engine of the anxiety. Looking at past experience to question this thought is really effective.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

The absence of effective study strategies strengthens the anxiety — not knowing "how to study" is itself a source of stress. A structured study plan can itself be therapeutic.

✋ Occupational therapist / Sensory perspective

Sensory factors like light and sound in the exam room can create extra anxiety in some students — knowing this in advance is helpful.

5

Practical technique: Visualization

When I first worked with this technique with Kamilə, I myself approached it with a bit of doubt — "close your eyes, imagine a successful exam" seemed too simple. But the result surprised me.

Here's how it's applied. I ask the client to close their eyes and imagine, step by step, walking into the exam room, sitting calmly, reading the questions and comfortably writing the answers. Details matter — what the desk looks like, the pen in their hand, the rhythm of their breathing. When this exercise is repeated a few times, the brain registers this scenario as "familiar" — this feeling of familiarity reduces anxiety in the real exam. The brain makes little distinction between imagination and real experience — this is a principle athletes have also used for many years.

Attention: Visualization should cover not just the positive outcome, but also encountering and managing a difficulty — for example, "a hard question came up, I took a deep breath, I continued." Only the picture of "everything will be perfect" doesn't reflect a realistic expectation.
6

Sample dialogue (with Kamilə, cognitive restructuring)

Therapist:Kamilə, what thought comes to you most before an exam?
Teen:"If I can't do this, everything will be over."
Therapist:Has this thought come true in your previous exams?
Teen:No, actually... I've always found some way.
Therapist:What does that tell you about this thought?
Note: With younger students, this questioning is done in simpler, more image-based language — a question like "does this thought help you, or get in your way?" is enough. At the university-student age, a more analytical, fact-based questioning can be used.

8

Guidance for parents and teachers

🚫 Don't compare

Comparisons like "look how easily so-and-so prepares" increase anxiety, decrease self-esteem.

💬 See the effort, not the outcome

"I'm proud of how you prepared" — this is support for the process, not the outcome.

😴 Protect sleep and routine

Sleeplessness before an exam increases anxiety and difficulty concentrating.

Homework: This week, have the client practice visualization for 3-5 minutes every day — in a quiet place, eyes closed, picturing a successful exam scene step by step. We'll discuss this experience together next session — what worked, what was hard.
9

Kamilə's story: the perfect student's hidden fear

The highest grades in the class register were next to her name. So why did Kamilə stay in bed with a stomach ache before every exam? This question led me into the depth of her story.

📋 Presentation

Kamilə was an 11th-grade student, 3 months from her graduation exam. She studied excellently, but before every mock exam she experienced insomnia, stomach ache, and a feeling of "my mind goes blank." Her mother was worried: "My daughter knows everything, but at the exam desk it's like she forgets it all."

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that Kamilə's anxiety didn't come from a lack of knowledge, but from the belief "if I'm not good enough, I'll disappoint my family." This belief created a state of excessive arousal, which temporarily "locked" her working memory — a classic test-anxiety pattern.

Therapist:Kamilə, what's the first thought that crosses your mind when you walk into the exam room?
Client:"If I get a bad result, everyone will be disappointed in me."
Therapist:What do you feel in your body when this thought comes?
Client:My heart pounds hard, my hands shake, it's like I forget everything.
Therapist:That makes a lot of sense — your body is sending a danger signal, as if a lion were in front of you. But there's no lion there, just a test sheet. Let's question this thought a bit: how many exams did you take last year?
Client:A lot... maybe more than 30.
Therapist:In how many of them was your family really "disappointed" in you?
Client:None... actually.
Core principle: In high-performing students, the root of exam anxiety is very often not incompetence, but a sense of conditional worth built around "being good enough."

🎯 How I apply this in session

Working with Kamilə, I used the evidence-seeking technique a lot — gathering real information about the past is far more effective at dismantling catastrophic future scenarios.

Assessment scales

📊

Westside Test Anxiety Scale

A brief, 10-item tool suited to the school context, quickly assessing the severity of exam anxiety.

📊

TAI (Test Anxiety Inventory)

Gives a more detailed profile by separating anxiety into "worry" and "emotionality" components.

📊

GAD-7

I use this in parallel to check whether generalized anxiety extends into life areas beyond exams.

Note: Kamilə's Westside score was in the "high anxiety" range at the start of treatment; after 5 sessions it dropped into the "moderate" range — the somatic symptoms during mock exams decreased in parallel too.
13

Clinical threshold crossing in exam anxiety

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Most cases in an exam anxiety program are situational, but the following signs may point to a broader anxiety or mood disorder:

  • The anxiety starting months in advance rather than just on exam day, turning into functional paralysis;
  • The emergence of self-harming behaviors to avoid the exam (deliberately getting sick, self-harm);
  • A deepening of hopelessness and worthlessness expressions related to academic pressure;
  • Somatic symptoms (insomnia, loss of appetite) persisting long-term, not limited just to the exam period.
  1. If self-harming behavior is detected, pause the exam-prep plan and move to a safety assessment.
  2. If expressions of hopelessness repeat, add a mood screening, inform the family.
  3. If somatic symptoms extend beyond the exam period, transition to a general anxiety screening (GAD-7/SCAS).
  4. If the family's academic expectations create additional pressure, directly discuss this dynamic in a parent session.

Note: Even though exam anxiety looks situation-specific, the underlying perfectionism or general anxiety structure is often broader.

  • Exam anxiety isn't a knowledge problem, it's a temporary block on cognitive resources.

  • Effective study strategies reduce procrastination and, in turn, anxiety.
