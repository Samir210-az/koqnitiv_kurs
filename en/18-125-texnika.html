Module 18 · Technique Collection

125 CBT Techniques

A broad collection of CBT techniques usable across every age group and presenting problem — a workbook organized by category.

📖

About this workbook

How to use it: This is the list of techniques I've built up over 18 years of practice and keep within reach during sessions. Every client is different, so I never run through the whole list in one session — I pick 2-3 techniques suited to the specific problem and the specific age, and keep the rest as an archive. I'd recommend the same to you: don't try to memorize this list, use it as something to come back to when you need it.

🧠 Cognitive Techniques

Techniques in this group work with thought — questioning it, reframing it. The first three are the ones I use most, especially with new clients.

001

Thought Record

Tracking automatic thoughts, emotions, and evidence in writing.

002

Evidence gathering

Listing the evidence for and against a thought.

003

Alternative explanations

Finding 2-3 different explanations for the same event.

004

Testing the catastrophe scenario

Comparing the "worst, best, most realistic" outcomes.

005

Naming cognitive distortions

Recognizing the error in a thought (labeling, overgeneralization, etc.).

006

What would you tell a friend?

Looking at your own situation from the outside, as a friend would.

007

Percentage technique

Rating belief in a thought 0-100%, then reassessing.

008

Responsibility pie

Dividing responsibility for an event among all the parties involved.

009

Downward arrow technique

Surfacing the core belief underneath a surface thought.

010

Schema mapping

Visualizing how core beliefs shape life.

011

Double standard technique

Checking whether you apply the same standard to yourself as to others.

012

Continuum line

Placing "all-or-nothing" thinking on a 0-100 line.

💪 Behavioral Techniques

Working with thought is one side, working with action is another. Sometimes a client thinks accurately but doesn't act — this group fills exactly that gap.

013

Activity scheduling

Reducing passivity by planning daily activities.

014

Pleasant activities list

Identifying concrete activities that lift mood.

015

Mastery/pleasure rating

Rating the sense of achievement and enjoyment after each activity.

016

Graded exposure

Step-by-step confrontation with fear- or anxiety-inducing situations.

017

Behavioral experiment

Testing a belief in real life.

018

Self-reward system

Giving a small reward after the target behavior.

019

Stimulus control

Changing environmental triggers of an unwanted behavior.

020

Activity chart

Tracking activity and mood hour by hour.

🧘 Relaxation and Body Skills

This group doesn't require words — it works through the body. It's especially my first choice with younger children who can't yet put their feelings into words.

021

Box breathing

Calming the nervous system with a 4-4-4-4 second breath cycle.

022

Diaphragmatic breathing

A deep, belly-breathing technique.

023

Progressive muscle relaxation

Tensing and releasing muscle groups in sequence.

024

Body scan

Directing attention sequentially across body parts.

025

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

Returning to "now" through the 5 senses.

026

Safe-place visualization

Picturing a calm, safe place in the mind.

027

Counting technique

Counting backward as a distraction (e.g., from 100 in steps of 7).

028

Cold water/temperature technique

Regulating the nervous system with cold water on the face.

😊 Emotion Regulation

Recognizing a feeling, naming it — it looks simple, but for many people it's the hardest skill of all.

029

Emotion thermometer

Rating the intensity of a feeling on a 0-10 scale.

030

Feelings vocabulary

Building precise expression through a broader emotional vocabulary.

031

Emotion wave

Visualizing that feelings come and go, rather than staying fixed.

032

Opposite Action

Doing the opposite of what the emotion is urging (a DBT technique).

033

STOP technique

Stop-Take a breath-Observe-Proceed.

034

Calming box

A personal kit of sensory/soothing objects.

035

Journaling

Expressing feelings through free writing.

🗣️ Communication and Social Skills

This is the most in-demand group for teenage and adult clients — most of the tension in relationships actually comes from a communication gap.

036

"I" language

Using "I feel..." instead of accusatory "You...".

037

Active listening

Listening without interrupting, and affirming what's heard.

038

Assertiveness practice

Expressing your rights respectfully, without aggression.

039

Role-play

Rehearsing a difficult social scenario in a safe environment.

040

The ability to say no

Setting a boundary and declining politely.

🎯 Problem-Solving and Goal-Setting

Sometimes all a client needs is to bring structure to a problem that feels chaotic. This group exists exactly for that.

041

Problem-solving steps

Define the problem → alternatives → decision → implementation → evaluation.

042

SMART goals

Setting specific, measurable, achievable goals.

043

Decisional balance

Comparing the pros and cons of a choice in a table.

044

Small-steps technique

Breaking a large goal into small, manageable steps.

👨‍👩‍👧 Family and Parent Tools

I give this group not to the client directly but to the parent or teacher — without ongoing support at home and in the classroom, progress made in the therapy room can fade quickly.

045

Positive reinforcement chart

Rewarding desired behaviors with points or stars.

046

ABC behavior tracking

Tracking what happens before and after a behavior.

047

Family meeting ritual

Scheduling a short weekly family conversation.

048

Specific praise

Naming a specific behavior instead of general praise.

🧩 Combining techniques in practice: Tugay's story

Each of the 125 techniques has value on its own, but the real skill is combining them correctly. Tugay's story shows what that looks like.

📋 Presentation

Tugay was 13, and avoided social events (birthdays, school functions), saying "everyone will laugh at me." A single technique wasn't enough here — this needed work with thought, with the body, and with taking real-world steps, all at once.

Therapist:Tugay, what goes through your mind right before a birthday party?
Client:"Everyone's going to look at me weird, I won't know what to say."
Therapist:Let's examine this thought with technique #005 — what kind of distortion is this?
Client:Mind reading, I guess — I don't actually know what they're thinking.
Therapist:Exactly. Now let's calm the body with #021 box breathing, then build a #016 graded exposure plan — short events first, then longer ones.
Core principle: The most effective treatment plan is usually not a single technique, but a purposeful combination of cognitive, body, and behavioral techniques.

📊 Matching the right assessment tool to the technique

After each technique group, here's a brief note on which measurement tool I use — the right tool helps guide the choice of the right technique.

📊

GAD-7 / SCAS

For tracking how effective cognitive and calming techniques are in anxiety-related work.

📊

PHQ-9 / BDI-II

For measuring the effect of Behavioral Activation techniques on depressive symptoms.

📊

SDQ

For tracking the overall functional impact of emotion regulation and social skills techniques.

🏠 Sample homework assignment

What I gave Tugay: this week, in one small social situation (like asking a shop assistant a question), write down the thought that comes up beforehand using technique #005, then apply #021 box breathing, and go through with the situation. Note the outcome, even if it goes badly, without judging it. The goal isn't a flawless performance, it's building experience.

A risk-informed approach to choosing a technique

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

None of the 125 techniques in this catalog can play the role of "treatment" during a crisis — the choice of technique always comes after a risk assessment, never before it:

  • An unexpectedly strong emotional reaction (dissociation, panic) arising during any technique;
  • Situations where cognitive restructuring techniques (e.g. #005) won't be sufficient on their own for a client with an active suicidal intent;
  • Using exposure-based techniques (fear hierarchies) with clients who have a medical contraindication;
  • The risk that Behavioral Activation techniques convert returning energy into action in severe depression (a paradoxical rise in risk).
  1. Before selecting any technique, take the current risk status (low/moderate/high) into account — the choice of technique should match that status.
  2. At high risk status, pause use of the technique catalog and move to stabilization and a safety plan.
  3. If an unexpectedly strong reaction arises, stop the technique immediately and move to grounding skills.
  4. When applying Behavioral Activation in depression, monitor rising energy alongside rising risk in parallel.

Note: A rich set of techniques doesn't replace clinical judgment — this catalog is a list of "what can be done"; the decision of "when it must not be done" always rests on the therapist's own assessment.
