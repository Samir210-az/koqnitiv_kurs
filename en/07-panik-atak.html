Module 07 · Panic Disorder

Panic Attacks & CBT

The first time Elnur came to me, he said "I thought I was going to die," describing his first attack on a bus. Ten sessions later, he covered 3-4 bus stops entirely on his own. This module is the map of that journey — a full CBT program for working with panic attacks, from children through adults. Panic attacks are extremely common, and most clients feel completely alone with it the first time they come in.

1

Clinical definition: Panic Disorder

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, panic disorder is characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks (a sudden, sharp peak of fear with physical symptoms — racing heart, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath) followed by at least 1 month of persistent worry about future attacks, or a behavior change (avoidance). Interoceptive exposure is one of the most effective treatment components.
In plain terms: A panic attack is your body ringing a "false alarm bell." Even without real danger, the body shifts into "fight or flight" mode: the heart races, breathing shallows, the head spins. These sensations feel very frightening, but they aren't dangerous — they pass on their own within a few minutes.

Most of my clients ask the same question the first time they come in: "Isn't this a heart attack?" My answer is always the same — no. The symptoms can look similar, but a panic attack passes, usually within 10-20 minutes. A heart attack doesn't. Understanding this distinction is already a therapeutic step in itself.

2

The self-feeding cycle of panic

1

Physical symptom

Racing heart, dizziness

2

Catastrophic interpretation

"I'm having a heart attack!"

3

Intensified fear

A stronger physical reaction

Core intervention: Interoceptive exposure — recreating the body's symptoms under controlled conditions, to experience that they're safe. The client sees this through their own body, not just words — this is the most convincing method there is.
3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children

  • Stomachaches, crying, clinging to a parent
  • Attempts to explain the symptom as an "illness"

🧑 Teens

  • Attempts to hide it in social settings, shame
  • More frequent in school/exam environments

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Avoidance of enclosed, crowded places (bus, market)
  • Limitations in work functioning
4

A multidisciplinary view

🧠 Psychological perspective

Catastrophic interpretation ("this is a heart attack") is the main engine of panic — misreading the body's signal.

✋ Occupational therapist / sensory perspective

During hyperventilation, the body feels "lost" — sensory tools like a weighted blanket or deep pressure can help with calming.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

Panic attacks that occur in exam settings can be reduced by coordinating with the teacher and arranging accommodations (extra time, a quiet room).

5

Practical technique: Interoceptive Exposure step by step

When I explain this technique for the first time, clients are often surprised — "why would I want to speed up my own heart?" The answer is simple: when we experience the sensation we fear in a controlled way, the body learns that it's safe.

Here are the steps. First, I identify together with the client which physical symptom creates the most fear — racing heart, shortness of breath, dizziness. Then we deliberately create that symptom with a safe activity (running in place, breathing through a straw, spinning the head). As soon as the symptom appears, we apply the trained breathing technique and watch together as the symptom passes on its own.

Note: This technique isn't applied without a doctor's clearance in clients with medical conditions like heart disease or asthma. I always check the medical history first, every time.
6

Sample dialogue (with Elnur, interoceptive practice)

Therapist:Elnur, run in place for 30 seconds, then stop.
Client:(runs, stops) My heart raced a lot.
Therapist:Does this feel similar to what happens during panic?
Client:Yes, very similar!
Therapist:Now use your breathing technique and see what happens.
Note: This method is very effective with adults, since they can grasp the abstract explanation. With children, I present the same principle as a game — like "who can run the fastest."

9

Guidance for loved ones

🚫 Don't say "calm down"

This is ineffective and makes the person feel misunderstood. "I'm right here, this will pass" is more helpful.

🫁 Model the breathing together

During a panic episode, the person nearby can model slow breathing themselves.

🏃 Don't make avoidance easier

Going in their place every time, or pulling them away from enclosed spaces, gives short-term relief but reinforces avoidance.

10

Homework

Practice for this week: If the client has a panic attack, have them note when, where, and how long it lasted in a small journal. The goal isn't to change anything — just to observe. Often this itself brings some relief, because it creates a sense of control. After a few weeks, we look at these notes together and identify patterns.
➕

Elnur: behind the thought "I'm having a heart attack"

After his first attack, Elnur had called emergency services three times. No problem was ever found with his heart. But the fear remained, because the body wasn't lying — it was just sending the wrong signal.

📋 Presentation

Elnur was a 21-year-old university student, and had his first panic attack during exam season — his heart raced, his breath was cut off, his hands went numb. From that moment on he lived with the fear "it'll happen again," avoiding being alone, sometimes even avoiding the metro.

🔍 What we found

Once the Panic Journal was filled out, it became clear that Elnur was interpreting bodily sensations (increased heart rate, dizziness) as "danger signals" — this is the classic panic cycle called catastrophic misinterpretation. Bodily sensation → frightening thought → more physical symptoms → stronger fear.

Therapist:Elnur, when your heart races, what's the first thought that comes to mind?
Client:I'm having a heart attack, I'm going to die.
Therapist:How many times have you had this thought so far?
Client:At least 15-20 times, in the last two months.
Therapist:And how many times has it actually been a heart attack?
Client:Never... but it feels completely real in that moment.
Therapist:I understand, it feels very real. This is your body's "false alarm system" — there's no real danger, but the system fires as if there were.
Key observation: In Elnur's case, the panic cycle was fed by interpreting bodily sensations as catastrophic. Changing that interpretation (together with interoceptive exposure) formed the foundation of treatment.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Elnur, we artificially created dizziness right in session by spinning briefly in place — the goal was to show that this sensation itself isn't dangerous, just uncomfortable.

CBT assessment tests and scales

In panic disorder, these tools are useful both for diagnostic clarity and for tracking progress.

📊

PDSS (Panic Disorder Severity Scale)

The clinical standard for measuring the frequency, severity, and functional impact of panic attacks.

📊

BSQ (Body Sensations Questionnaire)

Measures the level of fear of bodily sensations.

📊

ACQ (Agoraphobic Cognitions Questionnaire)

Assesses the frequency of catastrophic thoughts that arise during panic.

📊

ASI (Anxiety Sensitivity Index)

Measures general sensitivity to anxiety sensations — a predictive factor for panic.

📊

PHQ Panic module

A brief screening tool for whether panic attacks meet DSM-5 criteria.

Note: Elnur's BSQ and ASI scores dropped significantly over 8 weeks — showing that not just the frequency of attacks, but his general sensitivity to them, had decreased too.
🏠

Sample homework

Here's the specific assignment I gave Elnur.

Once a day, at a time of your choosing, run in place for 30 seconds or climb the stairs quickly. You'll feel your heart race — this is exactly the same sensation you feel during panic. The difference is: you created this yourself, you know why it's happening. Note the result: how many seconds did it take for your heart to calm down? This will show you that your body can regulate itself, even without your help.

11

Telling a panic attack apart from a dangerous physiological state

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

A panic attack program requires diagnostic clarity above all else — before moving to psychological intervention, the following signs require a medical rule-out:

  • Chest pain that continues at rest, not just with physical exertion;
  • Episodes of complete loss of consciousness (fainting) — rare in panic attacks, may point to a cardiac or neurological issue;
  • A family history of early-onset heart disease;
  • Self-harm or hopelessness expressions emerging alongside an increasing frequency of attacks.
  1. On a first attack episode, even if it presents as a classic panic attack, require a pediatric/cardiac rule-out.
  2. Once medically cleared, move on to box breathing and somatic education components.
  3. If fainting or persistent chest pain is identified, stop the session and make an urgent medical referral.
  4. If increasing attack frequency coincides with worsening mood, add a depression/hopelessness screening.

Note: A panic attack itself isn't physically dangerous, but a medical rule-out must be completed before telling the client that — otherwise credibility is lost.
