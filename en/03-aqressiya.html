Module 03 · Emotional Regulation

Aggression and CBT

"Is my child a bad kid?" I hear this question from nearly every family in the first session. My answer never changes — no. Below is the 10-session road map I've built over the years: viewed through psychological, sensory-integration, educational, and communicative lenses, each in its own place.

1

Scientific definition: the clinical model of aggressive behavior

Clinical definition: According to Dollard and Miller's "Frustration-Aggression" theory, aggression is behavior carrying intent to harm, arising in response to something blocking a goal. Clinically, a distinction is drawn between reactive (emotional, impulsive) and proactive (planned, instrumentally used) aggression. CBT intervention focuses on building emotion-regulation skills, cognitive restructuring, and social problem-solving skills.
In plain terms: I always compare aggression to a volcano. Pressure — tension, frustration, exhaustion — builds up slowly. If it doesn't "vent" in time, it comes out as an eruption. This, more often than not, isn't a sign of a "bad kid." It's simply the release valve for unregulated emotional pressure that's been building up.

My goal has never been to punish aggression. It's to find the need underneath it, the unregulated emotion — and point toward a healthier way out. With a client named Elvin, that's exactly the question we started with: what was happening in his body right before he hit someone?

2

Scientific view: types of aggression

I can't build a program without knowing which type of aggression I'm working with. The clinical literature divides it into several forms.

💥

Reactive aggression

An impulsive, emotional outburst in response to an immediate provocation. Hitting back the instant someone pushes you, for example.

🎯

Proactive aggression

Goal-directed and planned. Used instrumentally to obtain something — hitting someone to take their toy, for instance.

🗣️

Verbal aggression

Attack through words: insults, threats, shouting.

👊

Physical aggression

Expressed through bodily action — hitting, pushing, throwing objects.

🕸️

Relational aggression

Social exclusion, spreading rumors, "kicking someone out of the friend group." I see this especially often in adolescents.

3

What lies at the root of aggression? — A multidisciplinary view

Aggression almost never arises from a single cause. I've seen this repeatedly across 18 years of practice — building the right program requires looking through several different specialist lenses.

🧠 Psychological view

A gap in emotion-regulation skill, the "Frustration-Aggression" theory — the likelihood of aggression rises whenever something blocks a desired goal. Learned behavior shouldn't be forgotten either: once a child has seen that aggression gets them what they want, the behavior starts reinforcing itself.

✋ Occupational therapy / sensory integration view

In some children, aggression is actually the result of "sensory overload" — too much sensory input from noise, light, crowding. Sometimes it's the reverse — "sensory seeking," a need for movement, for pressure. When the body can't regulate itself, a behavioral eruption follows.

🗣️ Speech therapy / communication view

Children with speech and language difficulties, unable to put their needs into words, show their frustration physically instead — hitting, biting. The equation "communication difficulty = aggression risk" holds especially true at a young age.

🏫 Educational-psychology view

Learning difficulties, a sense of academic failure, a task that's too hard (or, just as often, too easy), lack of proper structure in the classroom — all of these can increase aggressive behavior.

4

How it looks different across age groups

The same unregulated emotion shows up wearing three different faces at three different ages.

👶 Children (ages 3–10)

  • Hitting, biting, pushing, throwing objects
  • Screaming, lying on the floor crying (tantrums)
  • Conflicts around difficulty sharing

🧑 Adolescents (ages 11–17)

  • Verbal attacks, insults, slamming doors
  • Defying rules, rebelliousness
  • Aggression through social media (cyberbullying)

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Passive-aggressive behavior (sarcasm, stonewalling)
  • Anger outbursts, family/work conflicts
  • Signs of physical tension (headaches, muscle tightness)
5

The CBT cycle of anger

Aggression isn't a one-moment eruption. It's the last link in a chain. Understanding that is the key to breaking it.

Intervention point: I teach the client to "pause" at any point in this cycle. But the most effective one is the "body reaction" stage — the moment before behavior kicks in, when a pause is still possible.

7

Practical technique: "Stop – Think – Act"

A simple, memorable three-step strategy. Used the moment an anger signal is felt — the first time I used it myself, I'd honestly made more of a promise to myself than to the client that keeping it this simple would actually work.

🛑

1. Stop

Halt the action, take 3 deep breaths.

🤔

2. Think

"What am I feeling right now? Is there another way?"

✅

3. Act

Choose a calm, considered response — a word or an action.

8

Occupational-therapy recommendation: sensory regulation tools

For children with sensory needs, I usually recommend setting up a small "calm-down corner" at home — with tools like these:

🧸

Weighted blanket/pillow

Deep pressure calms the body.

🎧

Noise-canceling headphones

Reduces sensory overload.

🤏

Fidget tools

Keeping the hands occupied redirects attention.

🏃

Movement break

Jumping, wall push-ups (heavy work) discharge excess energy.

10

Sample dialogue (with a 10-year-old)

Therapist:What did you feel when your classmate took your toy?
Child:I got really mad, so I hit him.
Therapist:I understand, getting mad is completely normal. What signals did you notice in your body right before you hit him? How was your heart?
Child:My heart was pounding hard, my hands got hot.
Therapist:Great observation! See, that's exactly the moment your "thermometer" is climbing toward red. Next time you notice those signals, how could we press the "Stop" button?
Note: Work with Elvin began with exactly this question too — the body always gives a warning before the words do, we just have to learn to read it.
11

Guidance for parents and teachers

🚫 Don't answer aggression with aggression

Responding to shouting with shouting reinforces the pattern. Keep your tone calm.

🏅 Praise calm behavior

"You stayed calm this time, that was great" — positive reinforcement matters.

📐 Set clear, consistent rules

Children are provoked less in a predictable environment.

🔍 Track triggers

Keep a small log tracking which situations tend to lead to aggression.

Homework (for the parent): This week, catch a moment your child stayed calm, and tell them the same day: "You handled yourself really well that time." One sentence is enough — but repeat it.

What's behind the "problem child" label?

When I first met Elvin, his teacher introduced him this way: "irritable, aggressive, punishment doesn't fix him." Six sessions later I realized that wasn't the right question at all — the right question was "why," not "how."

📋 Presentation

Elvin was 11, and for four months had been getting into physical conflicts at school 2–3 times a week. There had been no such problem before. His parents were going through a divorce, and Elvin saw his father once a week. His teacher called him "manipulative," his parents said he was "seeking attention." Nobody had asked him: "what are you feeling?"

🔍 What we found

As the behavior observation log filled in, it became clear the aggression wasn't random — it was almost always triggered by someone taking his things without asking, or a feeling of "unfairness." But underneath was something deeper: Elvin was linking his parents' separation to his own being a "bad boy." The anger, in fact, was a call for help.

Therapist:Elvin, right before you hit him that time, what did you feel in your body?
Client:My chest got hot, my hands balled into fists, like I was about to explode.
Therapist:What thought crossed your mind in that moment?
Client:"Nobody respects me, everyone's against me."
Therapist:Whether or not that thought was true, it felt very real to you in that moment. Now — between that thought and hitting him, could there have been another choice?
Client:Maybe I could've gone and told the teacher something.
Therapist:Great idea. That's exactly the "moment of choice" you're describing — that's what we're working to find, that gap between the heat in your chest and the punch that follows.
Key observation: Elvin's aggression wasn't random — it was systematic, with concrete triggers. Punishment didn't change the pattern, because the pattern's root was emotional; the behavior was nothing more than a surface signal.

🎯 How I apply this in session

Elvin and I found the phrase "the gap before the punch" together — then in every session after that we worked on widening that exact gap. Recognizing the body signal is the first step, it comes before everything else.

CBT assessment tools and scales

I don't work aggressive-behavior cases without standardized instruments — subjective impressions alone can be misleading, especially when the parent's and teacher's accounts differ.

📊

CBCL (Child Behavior Checklist)

A standard, parent-completed instrument assessing behavioral and emotional problems across a broad spectrum.

📊

ECBI (Eyberg Child Behavior Inventory)

Separately measures the frequency of behavior problems and the parent's level of concern.

📊

Conners-3

A multi-informant assessment system covering behavior, attention, and aggression subscales.

📊

SDQ (Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire)

A brief screening tool measuring both difficulties and strengths.

📊

Overt Aggression Scale (OAS)

An observation chart logging the severity and type (verbal, against objects, physical) of aggressive episodes.

Note: For Elvin, both mother and father filled out the ECBI separately — the difference was telling: his father rated the episodes at home as less frequent. That gap itself became part of the treatment focus.
🏠

Homework example

This was the concrete assignment I gave Elvin.

Every time you feel that heat in your chest, don't do anything at first — just stop. Then count to three, take a deep breath. Only after that, decide: what will I do? Track this in a notebook — how many times you were able to "stop," how many times you weren't. Failure is data too, just as valuable as success. I told Elvin: the goal isn't to be perfect, it's to widen the gap a little more each time.

The escalation point of aggression

⚠️ When urgent intervention is required

While working on anger control, a therapist must always be able to tell these signs apart — they differ from ordinary therapeutic resistance and call for urgent intervention:

  • A concrete, targeted threat — a clear answer to who, how, when ("plan + means + time");
  • Repeated or escalating episodes of cruelty to animals;
  • Living in an environment with access to weapons (a hunting rifle, sharp tools within easy reach);
  • Aggression turned inward — self-punishment, signs of self-harm.
  1. Calmly and without judgment, ask directly whether there's been any thought of harming someone else or themselves.
  2. If the plan+means+time triad is uncovered, arrange an urgent joint psychiatric referral with the parent immediately.
  3. Have a concrete conversation with the parent about restricting access to weapons/sharp tools at home (means restriction) — don't delay this step until the end of the session.
  4. If signs of comorbid Conduct Disorder appear, refer for a broader psychiatric evaluation.
  5. Document every episode in writing, with date, identified factors, and steps taken.

Note: In reactive aggression, escalation usually happens within minutes — a therapist's ability to recognize this window is decisive for ending the session safely.

📌 Module summary

  • Aggression is the unmanaged expression of built-up emotional pressure — not a "bad character."
  • Reactive, proactive, verbal, physical, and relational aggression each call for different intervention.
  • Root causes can be psychological, sensory, communicative, or educational — a multidisciplinary assessment matters.
  • The Anger Thermometer and "Stop-Think-Act" are the most effective practical tools.
  • Sensory regulation tools (weighted blankets, fidgets, movement breaks) can reduce the physical roots of aggression.
  • Parent/teacher collaboration is the key to making behavior change stick.
