Module 10 · Sensory-Behavioral Feeding Program

15-Session Feeding Program

Nərmin's mother said in the first session: "She eats exactly five foods, she won't touch anything else." Nərmin was 5 years old. Fifteen sessions later, the list had grown to twelve — small, but a huge victory for the family. Below is a sensory-informed, behavior-based workbook program for picky eaters. This program takes time, but the result lasts, because it's built on a foundation of trust.

1

Clinical definition: The Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) spectrum

Clinical definition: Persistent food selectivity in children can clinically fall along the ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) spectrum — here, the restriction isn't about body image, it's linked to sensory sensitivity, a prior negative experience (choking, vomiting), or a general lack of interest. A multidisciplinary assessment (pediatrician, dietitian, occupational therapist, psychologist) forms the basis of the diagnostic process.
In plain terms: For some children, a food's taste, smell, or texture is felt more intensely than "usual" — this is about how the sensory system works differently, not "being spoiled." Our goal isn't to force the child, it's to help them gradually, through pressure-free steps, build a "friendship" with new foods.
Important note: This program doesn't set specific weight/calorie targets — the goal is to build healthy, low-distress eating habits and a positive family mealtime experience. For concerns related to physical development, collaboration with a pediatrician/dietitian is recommended.

I tell parents in the very first minute — this isn't a "force the child to eat" program. Quite the opposite. Forcing, in the long run, makes the relationship with food even worse. Here, we build trust, we don't rush. The result sometimes takes months to arrive, but it stays once it does.

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Early childhood (2-6)

  • Rejection based on color, shape, or texture
  • Fear of new food (food neophobia)

🧑 School age and teens

  • Embarrassment in social situations (birthdays, restaurants)
  • Attachment to a small number of "safe" foods

The core technique: The Sensory Approach Ladder

Getting acquainted with a new food always follows this sequence, without pressure. Each step happens in its own time — sometimes one rung takes several sessions, and that's entirely normal.

1

Look

2

Touch

3

Smell

4

Touch to lips

5

Taste

Important: At any rung, the child has the right to "spit it out" — that's not failure, it's protecting the feeling of safety. Working with Nərmin, she couldn't get past the fourth rung until the third month — I never rushed her, and eventually she was ready on her own. That waiting is itself part of the therapy.
5

Sample dialogue (with Nərmin, the "looking" step)

Therapist:Nərmin, look, this is broccoli. Let's just look at it, you don't need to do anything else.
Child:Its smell is bad.
Therapist:Okay, let's just see what it looks like. What's its color like to you?
Child:Green, like a tree.
Therapist:Exactly right! That's enough for today.
Note: The word "eat" is never used in conversation during the early rungs. Attention is directed only at safe observation — this preserves the child's sense of control.

7

Guidance for the family

🚫 Don't force

Rules like "you can't leave the table until you finish" backfire, worsening the relationship with food.

🍽️ Create a calm, neutral environment

Mealtime should be shared time, not conflict.

👨‍👩‍👧 Be a role model

The child observes and imitates the parent's own attitude toward food.

Homework: This week, have the family put a new food on the table — ask the child only to "look" at it, not taste it. A small step, a lot of patience.
8

Nərmin's story: the child afraid of broccoli

Here's a fact: most children with a picky-eating problem aren't "spoiled," they're children whose sensory system simply works differently. Nərmin's story reminded me of this once again.

📋 Presentation

Nərmin was 6 years old, and got by on 5-6 foods — pasta, bread, cooked chicken, a few kinds of crackers. Every time a new food appeared on the table, she'd cry, and sometimes have a gag reflex. According to the family, her doctor said she was "healthy," but the pediatrician referred her to us because of a slowdown on her growth curve.

🔍 What we found

The sensory assessment showed that Nərmin had heightened sensitivity in her touch and taste receptors — this isn't "being spoiled," it's a neurological difference. A new touch, smell, or appearance genuinely created an overwhelming stimulus for her nervous system. And the parent's "eat one more spoonful" pressure had already turned mealtime into a source of extra tension.

Therapist:Nərmin, what happens in your body when you see a new food?
Client:I don't know... my heart beats hard, sometimes my mouth just closes on its own.
Therapist:That's really important information. That's your body's "fear button" — it's telling you that you're not ready for something new yet.
Client:So I'm not a bad child?
Therapist:Not at all. Your body just works a bit differently from other children's — it's more sensitive. We'll learn this step by step, at your own pace.
Client:You won't force me to eat?
Therapist:I promise. Today we'll just look at the broccoli — you're not even required to touch it.
Core principle: Selective eating is very often the result of a difference in sensory processing. Force doesn't calm this system down — on the contrary, it turns mealtime into a danger signal.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Nərmin, I never once said "eat this" — I always said "let's look at this," "let's smell this." Keeping control fully in her hands is the foundation of the whole process.

Assessment tools

Assessment on the selective-eating/ARFID spectrum needs to be multi-pronged — psychological, sensory, and pediatric information are all needed.

📊

NIAS (Nine Item ARFID Screen)

A brief tool assessing the three subtypes specific to ARFID (low appetite, sensory sensitivity, fear-based avoidance).

📊

BAMBI (Brief Autism Mealtime Behavior Inventory)

A brief tool measuring problematic mealtime behaviors (repertoire restriction, ritualistic behavior).

📊

Sensory Profile

An assessment applied by an occupational therapist, mapping the child's sensory-processing patterns (taste, touch, smell).

📊

Growth Chart

A clinical marker tracked together with the pediatrician, showing whether weight/height gain matches age norms.

Note: I applied the NIAS with Nərmin's family in the first session, and my occupational-therapist colleague carried out the sensory profile separately — these two results together formed the basis of the treatment plan.
🏠

Sample homework

Here's the specific assignment I gave Nərmin's family.

This week, put a "new acquaintance" food on the dinner table every evening — without demanding anything from the child. Just let it be there, let the family eat it themselves, and let the child look at it if they want, or not look at all if they don't. Note the child's reaction in the journal with one word: "looked," "moved away," "asked a question." The goal isn't the outcome, it's the gradual building of a sense of safety.

Signs of psychological risk in a feeding program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

A feeding/emotional-eating program mainly focuses on behavioral regulation, but the following signs may indicate a shift toward the eating-disorder spectrum, and should change the program's direction:

  • The emergence or intensifying of restriction (deliberately refusing food) behavior;
  • Excessive preoccupation with body weight, repeated mirror-checking behavior;
  • Post-meal self-blame and secretive eating episodes (binge behavior);
  • A sharp, unexplained change in weight (gain or loss).
  1. If signs of restriction or bingeing are detected, immediately supplement the program with an eating-disorder screening (e.g. the SCOFF questionnaire).
  2. If a sharp weight change is detected, refer to a pediatrician/nutrition specialist — this goes beyond this program's independent scope.
  3. If concern about body perception is strong, avoid giving exact numbers/calorie information — this can backfire.
  4. If there's conflict around food in the family environment, teach neutralizing strategies in the parent session.

Note: A feeding program and eating-disorder treatment are different frameworks — recognizing the boundary in time prevents the client from falling into a restriction spiral.
