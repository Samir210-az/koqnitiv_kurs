Module 04 · Emotional Regulation

Anxiety, Emotional Tension and Fear

Nihad told me in our first session: "There's a radio in my head, and it never turns off." I've remembered that line for years, because I've rarely heard a better description of anxiety. Below is a 10-session program that adapts from young children to adults — think of it less as a rigid rulebook and more as a flexible frame. Every family moves at a different pace, and I'm ready for that every time.

1

Scientific definition: Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Clinical definition: According to the DSM-5-TR, generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) is excessive, hard-to-control anxiety about a range of events or activities. It must be present on most days over a minimum of 6 months. Muscle tension, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, sleep disturbance — these are the accompanying somatic symptoms. In children it often shows up as performance anxiety, separation anxiety, or specific situational phobias.
In plain terms: Think of anxiety as an internal radio station. It's constantly broadcasting "but what if..." and "this isn't going to go well" messages. This radio exists to protect us. The problem is that sometimes it talks louder, and more often, than it needs to.

Fear and anxiety aren't the same thing, and I draw that distinction with every client, every session. Fear is concrete — like the reaction to a dog barking. Anxiety points toward the future, it's uncertain, and it's usually about something that hasn't happened yet. Once a client understands this distinction, they start seeing their own experience differently — a small step, but a powerful first one.

2

Clinical classification of symptoms

I look at anxiety through three lenses — they feed into each other, they're never experienced in isolation.

🧠

Cognitive

Persistent "what if" thoughts, attempts to control the future, indecisiveness, difficulty concentrating.

💗

Physical (somatic)

Stomachache, muscle tension, fatigue, sleep problems, a racing heart.

🚫

Behavioral

Avoidance, repeatedly seeking reassurance, procrastination, excessive over-preparation.

3

A multidisciplinary view

Anxiety isn't just something that lives "in the head." It lives in the body, in speech, in the classroom too — which is why I look at it through four different lenses every time.

🧠 Psychological view

Intolerance of uncertainty is one of the core engines of anxiety. The more a client wants to "know for sure," the stronger the anxiety grows — because life simply doesn't hand out certain answers.

✋ Sensory integration view

Persistent bodily tension can be tied to "sensory seeking" or "sensory sensitivity." Movement and deep pressure often calm the body — which is why I bring movement-based tools into at least one session.

🗣️ Speech therapy view

When young children can't put anxiety into words, it comes out as "body language" — a stomachache, crying. Working alongside a speech therapist colleague, in these cases we teach naming the feeling before naming the word.

🏫 Educational-psychology view

An environment under performance pressure — a highly competitive classroom, for instance — can intensify anxiety. Staying in touch with the teacher and adjusting task difficulty sometimes produces faster results than therapy itself.

4

Practical technique: Circle of Control

A large share of anxiety is directed at things we can't control. The first time I used this technique with Nihad, he was struck by "but I can't control the outcome" — and that's exactly the point, seeing that clearly.

✅

What I can control

My preparation, my behavior, my words

❌

What I can't control

Other people's opinions, the outcome, the weather

Assignment: The client picks one source of worry and sorts it into two columns — "in my hands" and "not in my hands." Then they're taught to direct energy only toward the first. The second column usually ends up longer the first time — which is itself a useful discovery for the client.

6

Sample dialogue (with a 13-year-old teenager)

Therapist:Nihad, describe your radio to me — when does it talk the loudest?
Teenager:The night before an exam. It starts up right when I'm trying to fall asleep.
Therapist:So exactly when you need it least, right when you just want to sleep. That's how it goes for a lot of people. Do you think it's possible to hear it without "agreeing" with it?
Teenager:I don't know... you mean just listen without believing it?
Therapist:Exactly that. The radio can keep talking — you're still the one who decides.
Note: With teenagers I "mature" the metaphor language a bit — instead of the colorful images I use with children, I choose a more analytical frame. The age gap really matters here.
7

Guidance for parents

🚫 Don't say "don't worry"

Instead, say "I understand you're worried, let's think about this together."

✅ Limit reassurance-seeking

Constant reassurance offers short-term relief but strengthens anxiety in the long run.

🗓️ Protect routine

A predictable daily rhythm provides a sense of safety.

Homework (for the parent): This week, in a moment your child is anxious, skip "don't worry" and just say "I'm right here with you." You'll see the difference yourself.
➕

Question: when does anxiety turn from "warning" into "prison"?

I asked myself this question often while working with Nihad. Finding the answer meant first looking closely at his everyday life.

📋 Presentation

Nihad was 13, known in his family as "the kid who worries about everything." He couldn't sleep before an exam, he'd assume "she's mad at me" if a friend's text reply was late, even the weather forecast could unsettle him. The family had seen a doctor — nothing physical was found.

🔍 What we found

His PSWQ and GAD-7 scores were in the moderate-to-high range. Conversations revealed that Nihad's core issue wasn't a specific fear — it was "intolerance of uncertainty." He wanted to know everything in advance, and whenever he couldn't, his mind filled in the worst-case scenario.

Therapist:Nihad, when you think about tomorrow's exam, what "movie" plays in your head?
Client:That I fail, everyone laughs, my parents are disappointed.
Therapist:When you see that movie, what do you feel in your body?
Client:My stomach turns, I can't fall asleep.
Therapist:And how many times has that movie actually come true, over the past year?
Client:Actually... never. It's always gone better than I expected.
Therapist:Interesting, isn't it? Our brain shows us a very convincing movie, but the statistics tell a different story.
Key observation: Nihad's anxiety was future-focused — it lived not in the present moment but in things that hadn't happened yet. That explains why the Worry Time technique proved especially effective for him.

🎯 How I apply this in session

Nihad and I built a simple tool called the "statistics notebook" — he wrote down the bad outcomes he predicted, then compared them against what actually happened. The numbers convinced him, not my words.

CBT assessment tools and scales

I use these tools in generalized anxiety work — both for the initial assessment and to track progress.

📊

GAD-7

The standard tool for a brief, quick assessment of generalized anxiety disorder.

📊

PSWQ (Penn State Worry Questionnaire)

A classic scale measuring the frequency of worry and how out of control it feels.

📊

SCARED

An assessment tool distinguishing between different types of anxiety disorders in children and adolescents.

📊

MASC-2

Separately measures the physical, social, and separation components of anxiety in adolescents.

📊

CASI (Child Anxiety Sensitivity Index)

Measures sensitivity to bodily sensations — useful for understanding the somatic side of anxiety.

Note: I repeated the PSWQ with Nihad every 3 sessions. The score dropped into the moderate range within 8 weeks — a real, tangible sign of progress, for both him and his family.
🏠

Homework example

This was the concrete assignment I gave Nihad.

Set aside a 10-minute "Worry Time" at the same time every day. Whenever anxiety shows up outside that window, tell yourself: "I'm noting this down, I'll look at it at 6pm." When the time comes, look back at what you wrote — most of it won't seem as important anymore. This isn't about suppressing anxiety, it's about giving it a boundary.

8

When anxiety crosses the clinical threshold

⚠️ When urgent intervention is required

In a generalized anxiety and fear program, most cases are manageable within the session, but certain signs call for an immediate expanded assessment:

  • Anxiety turning into functional paralysis — the child unable to leave home/their room for days;
  • Somatic complaints (stomachache, headache) rapidly increasing without a medical explanation;
  • Serious disruption to sleep and eating, accompanied by weight loss;
  • Expressions of hopelessness — recurring thoughts like "it's never going to get better."
  1. Repeat the assessment with the GAD-7 or SCAS, and document any sharp increase.
  2. If somatic complaints predominate, get a pediatric consultation first — offer the psychological explanation only after medical causes are ruled out.
  3. If functional paralysis (unable to attend school/leave the house) lasts more than 2 weeks, plan an expanded assessment together with the family.
  4. Don't hesitate to ask a direct, calm question about expressions of hopelessness.

Note: Anxiety and depression are highly comorbid in children — focusing only on fear symptoms and missing the mood component is a common clinical mistake.
