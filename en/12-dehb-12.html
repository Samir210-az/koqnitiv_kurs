Module 12 · Attention and Hyperactivity

ADHD — A 12-Session Program

Murad's teacher said at our first meeting: "He just doesn't try." I've heard this many times over the years, but I give the same answer every time — Murad does try, his brain just works differently. Twelve sessions later, Murad himself was able to tell his teacher, "give me a bit of time, I can do this." Below is a full, multidisciplinary, 12-session program for working with ADHD in children and teens. This program changes not just the child but the adults around them too — because when understanding changes, the relationship changes too.

1

Clinical definition: Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, ADHD is a persistent pattern of developmentally inappropriate inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms that begins before age 12 and is observed in at least two settings (home, school). Per Barkley's executive function theory, ADHD is mainly linked to a developmental delay in self-regulation, working memory, and inhibitory control. Three subtypes are distinguished: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, and combined type.
In plain terms: ADHD is the brain's "attention steering wheel" working differently. It's not laziness or bad parenting — it's a developmental difference in the brain's executive functions (planning, sustaining attention, impulse control). As Dr. Edward Hallowell put it — this brain has a Ferrari engine, but runs on bicycle brakes. There's power, but the control hasn't fully matured yet.

I tell parents in the first session — there's a big difference between "he can't" and "he won't." A child with ADHD very often wants to, plans to, even promises to. But the executive function — the mechanism that turns intention into action — hasn't fully matured yet. Understanding this completely changes the parent's reaction. Years ago I hadn't fully grasped this difference myself, until working with a client I saw that the question "why don't you" never accomplished anything — when the question changed, the outcome changed too.

2

A multidisciplinary view

✋

Occupational therapist

Regulating sensory-movement needs (fidget tools, movement breaks). Moving increases focus for some children, not the opposite.

🏫

Educational psychology

Classroom accommodations — seating spot, short breaks, visual support. Small changes can make a big difference.

🧠

Psychological

Self-regulation, frustration tolerance, self-esteem work — in a child who has heard years of criticism, self-esteem often needs special attention.

🗣️

Speech-language pathologist

Difficulty following instructions during inattention — this can be linked to language-processing speed, and needs a speech-language assessment.

3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children (ages 5-11)

  • Inability to stay seated, constant movement
  • Switching to another activity before finishing the task
  • Difficulty waiting for a turn

🧑 Teens

  • Hyperactivity looks more like inner restlessness
  • Difficulty with time management, procrastination
  • Low self-esteem, academic pressure

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Organizational difficulty, forgetfulness
  • Difficulty sustaining attention at work
4

Practical technique: Time Management and Task Breakdown

One of the biggest difficulties in a child with ADHD is a phenomenon called "time blindness" — not sensing how much time has passed. A visual timer fills this gap, because time is no longer abstract, it's something visible to the eye.

Here's how it's applied. I first break the big task into small, concrete parts — instead of "clean your room," "first put the books on the shelf," then "hang the clothes in the closet." A visual timer is set for each small part, usually 10-15 minutes. The moment the task is done, a small acknowledgment is given immediately — this helps the dopamine system work, because waiting for a reward is hard in ADHD, immediate feedback is more effective. When I explain this principle to parents, I often see that they'd previously used a "big reward, far in the future" system and hadn't seen results — this is exactly why.

Attention: Working with Murad, in the first attempt I broke the task into parts that were still too big — the result was unsuccessful. In the second attempt, when I made each step even smaller, success came. This taught me — the step we call "small" in ADHD often needs to be even smaller than we think.
5

Practical technique: Stop-Think-Act

A simple three-step strategy I use for working with impulsivity. The name looks simple, but applying it takes practice — like any new skill. I repeat these three steps with the child many times through role play, because a skill learned in a calm state is more easily remembered in a real, exciting moment.

🛑

1. Stop

Pause for a second before acting.

🤔

2. Think

"Is this the best choice?"

✅

3. Act

Choose the thought-through response.

Task: I recommend keeping these three steps on a colored card on the child's desk — a visual reminder is often more effective than a verbal one.
6

Sample dialogue (with Murad, task breakdown)

Therapist:Murad, finishing homework is hard for you, isn't it?
Child:Yes, I start, but I think about something else.
Therapist:I understand, that's not laziness. Let's break the task into small parts — first just the first question, then a break.
Child:Okay, that looks easier.
Therapist:Let's set a 10-minute timer, you'll see, you'll manage it.
Note: My words never carry a "why don't you" tone, always a "how can we" tone. This small difference prevents the child from going into a defensive stance. With teens, I shift this tone to a slightly more equal-level, partnership language — they prefer looking for the solution together, not being told what to do.

8

Guidance for parents and teachers

⏱️ Use visual time tools

Clocks and timers give a clearer message than words.

🧩 Break the task into parts

A big task is like an "invisible wall" for the ADHD brain.

🏅 See the strengths

Energy, creativity, spontaneity — these are also part of ADHD, not just the difficulty.

🚫 Don't label

Words like "lazy," "inattentive" damage self-esteem, they don't change behavior.

Homework: This week, have the parent/teacher break a daily task into three small parts, and give small acknowledgment after each part. We'll discuss the result at the next session. These notes themselves often show how small changes accumulate over a few weeks into a big difference.
9

Murad's story: dinosaurs in the notebook

Picture the classroom: the teacher explains at the board, 24 children listen, and one child draws a dinosaur in the margin of his notebook. That was Murad — and this child, who at first glance looked "inattentive," could in fact memorize dinosaur species for hours.

📋 Presentation

Murad was 9 years old, and finishing homework took him 3-4 hours, even though the task itself was 30 minutes' worth. His teacher complained that he constantly got up from his seat, interrupted. His mother said: "At home he reads a book about dinosaurs for hours, but gets tired after 5 minutes on a math task."

🔍 What we found

This is a classic ADHD pattern — not a lack of attention, but a problem of regulating attention. He experiences hyperfocus on an interesting topic (dinosaurs), and can't hold his attention on a task that seems uninteresting. A big task was like an "invisible wall" for his executive functions — he couldn't see where to start.

Therapist:Murad, what do you feel when you look at a math task?
Client:It looks really big. I don't know where to start, so I just sit and think about something else.
Therapist:And when you read the dinosaur book?
Client:I know that one, it's interesting, I don't notice how time passes.
Therapist:So your brain isn't lazy — it just gets confused with "big and unclear" tasks. Let's break the math task into small, clear parts, like the dinosaur book.
Client:What do you mean?
Therapist:Instead of 10 questions all at once, we split it into 2 questions — a break — 2 questions. In each break you can do whatever you want for 2 minutes.
Core principle: The problem in ADHD isn't the absence of attention, it's the inability to regulate attention. Hyperfocus on an interesting topic, and complete distraction on an uninteresting one — these are two faces of the same brain.

🎯 How I apply this in session

Working with Murad, I never once said "pay attention" — instead, I fit the task into a format his brain could work with. When the language changes, the behavior changes too.

Assessment scales

In ADHD assessment, gathering parent and teacher information together is important — behavior can look different across different settings.

📊

Vanderbilt ADHD Rating Scale

A widely used tool with parent and teacher versions, assessing ADHD symptoms and co-occurring problems (anxiety, behavior).

📊

Conners' Rating Scale (CRS-3)

An assessment tool giving a detailed profile of attention, hyperactivity, and executive functions.

📊

SNAP-IV

A brief, frequently applicable scale based on DSM-5-TR criteria, for tracking progress during treatment.

Note: There was a difference between Murad's parent and teacher Vanderbilt assessments — this showed me that the lack of structure in the classroom had more impact than at home. I directed the intervention toward the school environment first.
13

Comorbidity and risk screening in an ADHD program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Although the main focus of the 12-session ADHD program is attention/impulsivity, the following signs point to a comorbid condition and require the program to be expanded:

  • Impulsivity turning into dangerous behavior (running into the street without thinking, taking risks from heights);
  • Deep self-esteem deterioration and expressions of hopelessness linked to persistent academic failure;
  • Emotional-regulation difficulty shifting into anger outbursts, signs of conduct disorder;
  • Side effects related to medication (insomnia, loss of appetite) not being tracked.
  1. If dangerous impulsive behavior is detected, plan concrete safety measures together with the parent (supervision, environmental changes).
  2. If self-esteem deterioration deepens, conduct an additional mood screening (an age-appropriate form of the SDQ or PHQ-9).
  3. For clients on medication, briefly ask about side effects at every session, and share any significant finding with the prescribing physician.
  4. If anger outbursts cross into the territory of an aggression program, discuss the need for parallel intervention with the family.

Note: The emotional-regulation component of ADHD is often overlooked — focusing only on attention/impulsivity can miss the underlying difficulty.

📌 Module summary

  • ADHD is a developmental difference in executive functions (planning, attention, impulse control), not laziness.
  • Breaking a task into small parts and giving immediate acknowledgment are the most effective practical tools.
  • Stop-Think-Act is a simple, applicable strategy for working with impulsivity.
  • Multidisciplinary support (occupational therapist, educational psychology, speech-language pathologist) completes the full picture.
  • Seeing the strengths, not labeling, is important for long-term self-esteem.
