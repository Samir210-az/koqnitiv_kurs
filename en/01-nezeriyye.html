Module 01 · Foundational Theory

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy Theory

I've been sitting behind this desk for eighteen years, and I still notice something new every time. This is one of my favorite modules to teach — because everything else builds on it. If this foundation isn't solid, the other nineteen modules end up floating in the air. Let's build it together. Whatever brought you here — a working clinician, a student still in training, or someone simply curious about the field — this module is for you.

1

What is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy?

Let me ask you something: why does the same event send two people down completely different paths? One cries, the other shrugs it off. That's exactly where CBT starts — it's not the event itself but what we tell ourselves about it that runs the show. It's also one of the most extensively researched, most rigorously tested approaches in all of therapy.

🎯 The core idea

I explain it to my students the same way every time: event — filter — outcome. The filter is your thinking. Two people hear the exact same remark; one gets angry, the other laughs — because their filters are different. That's really all there is to it. And yet that's the key to the whole approach.

Example: Two colleagues of mine once got the same piece of criticism from our boss on the same day. One went home and cried that evening. The other said "fine, I'll fix it" and moved on. The difference wasn't in the event. It was in the internal dialogue running through their heads.

🎯 How I apply this in session

In the very first session I usually tell that same story — why two people can react so differently to one thing. Once the client hears it, they take a step back from "there's something wrong with me."

The misconception I run into most often: The goal of CBT isn't to swap negative thoughts for positive ones. The goal is to examine how accurately a thought reflects reality and arrive at a more balanced conclusion. I stress this to every new student, because confusing CBT with "just think positive" is by far the most common mistake I see. I never tell a client "think something nice instead." I ask: how accurate is this thought? What evidence supports it, what evidence doesn't? Sometimes the outcome is more positive — sometimes it's just more realistic and more bearable. Those are two different things.
2

Historical development

CBT grew out of two parallel theoretical lines converging: Albert Ellis's Rational Emotive Therapy and Aaron Beck's Cognitive Therapy.

1950sWhen Albert Ellis said that it isn't events that upset people but the irrational beliefs they hold about them, plenty of people were skeptical. He called his approach Rational Emotive Therapy.
1960sA few years later, while working with depressed patients, Aaron Beck noticed something curious: a rapid stream of negative thoughts running through his patients' minds, most of which they barely noticed. He called these "automatic thoughts" — a term still used today.
1970s–1980sBeck turned this observation into a simple, teachable model. Years later that model would become the most extensively researched approach in all of psychotherapy.
1990s to todayFrom there CBT expanded well beyond depression — into OCD, panic, and populations ranging from young children to teenagers. The "third wave" approaches — ACT, DBT, mindfulness-based CBT — all grew out of this same root.

🎯 How I apply this in session

Honestly, I don't recite the history to clients. But I do remind my students constantly — this wasn't dreamed up overnight by one person. It survived decades of testing to get here.

3

The cognitive triangle: Thought – Emotion – Behavior

This triangle hangs on my office wall, because I come back to it in nearly every session. Thought, emotion, behavior — all three are connected. Touch one, and the other two move too.

Clinical application: When deciding where to start an intervention, I first look at where the client seems most "stuck." Sometimes I start by questioning the thought (cognitive restructuring), sometimes simply by shifting the behavior — for instance, having someone afraid to leave the house take one small step outside. Either one moves the whole triangle.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I sketch this triangle on paper and put it in front of the client. Then I just ask: which corner do we start from today — thought or behavior? Often they answer that themselves.

4

The ABC Model (Albert Ellis)

Ellis's model is simple but powerful: it isn't the Activating event (A) that upsets us — it's the Belief (B) we build around it. That belief is what produces the Consequence (C). That's the whole model.

A

Activating Event

Trigger: my manager walked past without a glance.

B

Belief

Belief: "That means she's unhappy with me, she's going to let me go."

C

Consequence

Consequence: a knot in the stomach, anxiety all day, avoiding work.

Therapist:Kamran, what exactly went through your mind the moment your manager walked by?
Client:She didn't even look at me. That means she's not happy with my work.
Therapist:What made you land on that — was there a specific piece of evidence?
Client:Actually... nothing. She just walked past.
Therapist:Could there be another explanation?
Client:Maybe she was in a hurry. There was a morning meeting that day.
Therapist:So at least two different readings of the same event are possible. See that? A different belief produces a completely different feeling.

🎯 How I apply this in session

Just like with Kamran — I never say "your thought is wrong." I ask questions, and he finds it himself. A belief you arrive at yourself sticks far better than one I hand you.

5

Three levels of cognitive structure

According to Beck, our thoughts are organized in three layers, from surface to depth. I call it "the three layers of the iceberg," because the surface layer is visible, while the deeper ones have to be uncovered.

1️⃣ Automatic thoughts

These are the uninvited guests that show up in our minds on their own. Right before an exam, the thought "I'm not going to pass this" flashes through — we barely even register it, we just feel its effect. Children often struggle to put these thoughts into words, so I frequently have them draw instead, to give the thought a shape. In teenagers the process moves faster, since abstract thinking has already developed.

2️⃣ Intermediate beliefs (rules, assumptions)

These are hidden rules in an "if... then..." form. For example: "If I'm not perfect, no one will love me." A person often has no idea they're carrying such a rule — yet it's exactly what's steering their behavior.

3️⃣ Core beliefs (schemas)

This is the deepest layer. Beliefs like "I'm worthless" or "The world is dangerous." These usually date back to childhood, and they're the slowest to shift — though never impossible. I've seen that happen again and again over the years.

Therapist:Right before an exam, what exactly goes through your mind?
Client:The thought "I won't be able to do this" comes up.
Therapist:And what rule sits underneath that thought — in an "if... then..." form?
Client:I guess... something like "if I'm not perfect, I've failed."

🎯 How I apply this in session

First we catch the automatic thought, then I ask — what rule is underneath it? Sometimes three or four questions later we land right on the core belief, and the client is surprised themselves.

6

Cognitive distortions

These aren't learned overnight. I'll admit — even after all these years, I still catch a few of them in my own thinking sometimes. Recognizing them is one of the most fundamental skills in CBT.

All-or-nothing thinking
"If I'm not perfect, then I'm a complete failure."
Overgeneralization
"I made a mistake once, so I always mess things up."
Catastrophizing
"My heart is racing — I must be having a heart attack!"
Mind reading
"He thinks I'm stupid."
Fortune telling
"This meeting is definitely going to go badly."
Emotional reasoning
"I feel like a failure, so I must actually be one."
"Should" statements
"I should never make mistakes."
Labeling
"I'm a total failure" (one action turned into an identity).
Mental filtering
Fixating on the one critical comment out of ten positive ones.
Personalization
"The meeting went badly because I was there."
Client:If they don't like the project, that means I can't do my job at all.
Therapist:Which pattern does that sound like to you?
Client:Probably... all-or-nothing thinking.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I don't have clients memorize this list. I just ask, when I hear their own sentence: "which pattern does that resemble?" What they find themselves sticks far deeper than a label I hand them.

7

Core principles of CBT

🤝 Collaborative empiricism

I never tell a client "this thought of yours is wrong." We're both detectives — we lay the thought on the table as a hypothesis to be tested against evidence.

🎯 Goal-directed and structured

Every session has an agenda. An unstructured session is wasted time — I say that from experience.

⏱️ Time-limited (short-term)

Usually 8–20 sessions. Dragging on tends to be a problem in itself — CBT's strength lies precisely in fast, concrete results.

❓ Socratic questioning

I never hand over a ready-made answer. I ask: "What made you land on that?", "Could there be another explanation?", "In the worst case, what would happen, and how would you cope?" These questions open a path for the client to reach their own answer.

📓 Homework and thought records

Something learned in the room doesn't stick unless it's tried out at home. I end every session with one small, concrete assignment — not a big one, but a real one.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I don't list these five principles out loud to the client — I just demonstrate them through how I behave. They're the quiet backdrop running behind everything I do in session.

8

Structure of a typical CBT session

Mood check-in

I ask on a 0-to-10 scale: how anxious have you been today? A simple question, but it tells you a lot.

Bridge — connecting to the last session

What stayed with you from last time? This question links each session to the last.

Agenda setting

What should we focus on today? We decide this together — I never set it unilaterally.

Homework review

Did you do it? If not, why not? This is one of the steps most often skipped — and one of the most important.

Work on the main topic

This is where a new technique is introduced, or an existing problem is worked through more deeply.

Summary and feedback

What was useful today? I need to hear this in the client's own words.

New homework

What should we try before next time? Small, concrete, measurable.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I don't run through these steps mechanically, but I never skip any of them either — especially reviewing the homework. That's exactly where a lot of therapists rush. I don't.

Practical worksheet: Thought Record

This worksheet never leaves my bag. It looks simple, but it's the tool I've used most for years — it teaches the client to track their own thinking in real situations.

SituationAutomatic thoughtEmotion (0–100)Evidence againstBalanced thought
Texted a friend, got no reply "She's angry with me, she doesn't want to be friends anymore" Anxiety — 80 She's replied late before too, her workplace is busy "She's probably just busy, this isn't necessarily about our friendship"
Classmates laughed while I was nearby "They're laughing at me" Shame — 75 They were looking at their phones, could've been about something else "Their laughter might not be about me at all"
Assignment (for the client): This week, log at least 3 situations in this table format. We'll go through them together next session.

🎯 How I apply this in session

The first time, I fill this table out together with the client, right there in the room, so they're not lost when doing it alone at home. Handing someone a blank table is the mistake I see most often.

10

Clinical case: Kamran's full story

Kamran was 34, a mid-level manager at a tech company. When he first came to me, his complaint was "constant anxiety and trouble focusing at work." At first glance it looked like ordinary work stress. But as we dug in, it turned out to run much deeper.

📋 Presentation

Kamran read every neutral action from his manager — not saying hello, walking past quickly — as "she's unhappy with me." This reading repeated several times a day, each time triggering hours of anxiety and avoidance at work. He'd been living like this for three months, his sleep was disrupted, and his performance had genuinely started slipping — his own fear was beginning to fulfill itself.

🔍 What we found

Over six sessions it emerged that Kamran's core belief — "if I'm not perfect, I'll be rejected" — traced back to childhood, to a highly demanding father. Every ambiguous signal at work reactivated that old belief. His manager not saying hello functionally became, without his realizing it, "father's disapproving look."

Outcome: After 12 sessions, Kamran developed the habit of asking himself, in the same kinds of situations, "is this backed by evidence, or is this an old pattern?" The anxiety didn't disappear entirely — I never promise that — but it dropped to a manageable level.

CBT assessment tools and scales

Before the first session, I generally use several standardized tools — a simple "how are you" question isn't enough, you need an objective starting point.

📊

Beck Depression Inventory (BDI-II)

The most widely used self-report scale for measuring the severity of depressive symptoms.

📊

Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI)

Measures the physical and cognitive symptoms of anxiety separately.

📊

Automatic Thoughts Questionnaire (ATQ)

Assesses the frequency of negative automatic thoughts.

📊

Dysfunctional Attitude Scale (DAS)

A scale measuring deep, dysfunctional core beliefs.

📊

GAD-7

A brief, fast screening tool for generalized anxiety disorder.

📊

PHQ-9

A depression screening tool, used constantly in everyday practice.

Note: These scales don't produce a diagnosis — they only measure severity and help track progress across treatment. I re-administer the BAI with Kamran every 4 sessions, so we can see progress in numbers too — sometimes a client sees progress on the page before they feel it themselves.
🏠

Homework (for you)

Before we close this module, I want to ask something of you. Today or tomorrow, find a small situation that's been bothering you — nothing dramatic, an ordinary moment of tension is enough.

Fill out a Thought Record for yourself. Write down the automatic thought, rate the emotion from 0 to 100, then ask yourself the same question I asked Kamran: "What made me land on this thought? Could there be another explanation?" If you don't try this exercise on yourself first, it'll be much harder to ask it of a client. I tell every one of my students this — yourself first, then the client.

Where risk screening fits into the assessment phase

⚠️ When urgent intervention is required

While this module covers theoretical foundations, every CBT program's first session should include structured risk screening — this is the general framework that the topic-specific protocols in every later module build on.

  • Any direct or indirect expression of self-harm or harm to others, from the client or a parent, at the first meeting;
  • A positive response on item 9 of the PHQ-9, item 9 of the GAD-7, or a similar item on any initial assessment tool;
  • A family history of psychiatric illness, suicide, or a current serious family crisis;
  • The clinician's own subjective sense of unease — a feeling that "something isn't right," even without a concrete statement to point to.
  1. Apply structured risk screening as standard protocol within the first 2 sessions with every new client, regardless of presenting topic.
  2. If screening is positive, draft a safety plan before moving forward with the treatment plan.
  3. Always document findings in writing, with the date noted — this matters both clinically and legally.
  4. When in doubt, don't hesitate to seek case consultation with a colleague — never decide alone.

Note: The topic-specific crisis protocols presented in each following module complement this general framework — they don't replace it.

📌 Module summary

  • CBT rests on the thought–emotion–behavior triangle, with all three interconnected.
  • Ellis's ABC model shows that it's the belief, not the event, that determines the emotional outcome.
  • Cognitive structure has three layers: automatic thoughts, intermediate beliefs, core beliefs (schemas).
  • Cognitive distortions — systematic errors in thinking — need to be recognized in both yourself and your client.
  • CBT is structured, goal-directed, collaborative, and time-limited — don't lose sight of that.
