Module 11 · Group Format

CBT Group Therapy Program

In my first group session, everyone was silent, no one raised their eyes. By the fourth session, when I heard laughter in the room, I understood — the group had started living its own life. Below is the full structure for running CBT in group format with people who share a similar problem. This format creates a completely different dynamic from an individual session, and I never forget that.

1

Professional definition: The Group CBT Model

Clinical definition: Group CBT is structurally similar to individual CBT, but is delivered with 6-10 participants, based on Irvin Yalom's theory of "therapeutic factors" (universality, instillation of hope, group cohesion, interpersonal learning). Research shows group CBT is as effective as individual therapy for many disorders (anxiety, depression, social phobia), at a lower cost.
In plain terms: Group therapy is several people facing a similar difficulty coming together and learning together under a therapist's guidance. Its greatest strength is creating the feeling "I'm not alone."

In an individual session, the client hears from me that the feeling is normal. In the group, they see it in someone else's eyes — that's a completely different kind of impact. There's a power here that can't be explained in words.

2

Advantages of the group format

🤝

Mutual support

Members learn from and inspire each other.

🎭

Social skill practice

A natural setting for role plays.

💰

Efficiency

The ability to help more people at the same time.

Important: Group size is usually optimal between 5-8 people, and members should be selected from a similar problem/age group. When the age gap is too wide, the dynamic can break down — in a teen group, the needs of a 12-year-old and a 17-year-old are very different.
3

A multidisciplinary view

🧠 Psychological perspective

Yalom's principle of "universality" — the feeling "I'm not alone" — is one of group therapy's most powerful curative factors.

🗣️ Speech-language / communication perspective

The group setting becomes a safe "practice field" for members with communication difficulties.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

In teen groups, structured activities (role play, worksheet exercises) hold attention better.

4

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children

  • Play- and drawing-based group activity is more effective
  • Attention span is short, sessions are split into short modules

🧑 Teens

  • Being with similar-aged peers itself carries a therapeutic effect
  • Structured activity helps hold attention

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • More open to deep discussion and mutual feedback
  • The group can turn into a long-term support network
5

Practical technique: Facilitating a group step by step

Running a group is a completely different skill from an individual session. In my early years, I didn't immediately notice one member talking too much and leaving no room for others — since that day, I've paid special attention to "speaking balance" in every session.

First, I build the group rules together with everyone — confidentiality, not interrupting, not judging. Then, in every session, I run a round-robin sharing so everyone gets a turn to speak. I use Socratic questioning differently in a group — instead of questioning one member's thought, I direct it to the whole group with "who thinks about this differently?", which gets everyone thinking. This method, especially in teen groups, prevents a rigid teacher-student dynamic from forming.

Attention: A member should never be embarrassed in front of others in the group. Difficult topics are kept for individual sessions; in the group, we work on general principles.
6

Sample dialogue (within the group)

Therapist:Who had a new experience with their anxiety this week?
Leyla:I did. I tried the breathing technique before an exam, it worked.
Therapist:Great! Did anyone have a similar experience?
Vüsal:I tried it too, but it didn't fully work for me.
Therapist:That's a really useful observation — not every technique works the same for everyone. Let's think together about what could be different for Vüsal.
Note: In the group, I never declare one answer "correct." The question goes to the group, and the answer comes from the group too — my role is more about guiding.

8

Homework

This week's practice: Have each member try one technique (breathing, cognitive questioning, or another) in real life and briefly note the result. At the next session, these notes will form the basis of the round-robin sharing. Sometimes that very moment of sharing becomes the most valuable part of the whole session.
9

Aytac's story: from the shadows to the heart of the group

When starting group therapy, you can never know in advance which member will benefit the most. My experience with Aytac showed me this once again.

📋 Presentation

Aytac was 8 years old, and joined the group with a social anxiety diagnosis. For the first two sessions she barely spoke at all, she just drew and listened to others' conversations. Her mother said off to the side: "She talks a lot at home, but around people she says she swallows her tongue."

🔍 What we found

Something interesting happened in the third session — when Rəşad talked about his own fear, Aytac voluntarily asked to speak for the first time: "I feel that way too." This moment shows the true power of group therapy: sometimes it's not the therapist's words but another child saying "me too" that is the most powerful intervention.

Therapist:Aytac, how did what Rəşad said make you think?
Client:My heart pounds hard when I talk in class too, I thought it only happened to me.
Therapist:See that? You're not alone. Rəşad, what did you feel hearing that?
Client:(Rəşad) That's good, it means I'm not alone either.
Therapist:This is the group's most important lesson — everyone has their own fear, but sharing it makes the weight lighter.
Core principle: In group format, therapeutic change very often arises not from the therapist-client line, but from "me too" moments between members. The therapist's job is to recognize and strengthen these moments.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I never force quiet members like Aytac to speak — I just create small windows that open a chance for her within the natural flow of conversation. Patience is the hardest, but most important, part of group facilitation.

Assessment scales

In group therapy, both individual progress and the quality of the group's dynamic need to be tracked.

📊

SCAS (Spence Children's Anxiety Scale)

A reliable tool for individually tracking group members, measuring types of anxiety in children (social, separation, general).

📊

SDQ (Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire)

A tool assessing emotional and behavioral problems in brief form, with parent and teacher versions.

📊

GSRS (Group Session Rating Scale)

A brief feedback tool measuring group members' satisfaction with the session and group cohesion.

Note: Aytac's SCAS result showed high social anxiety at the start of the program; after the 6th session this score had dropped significantly — the "me too, I'm not alone" moments she experienced within the group were the main driving force behind this.
13

Signs of individual crisis in group therapy

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

By its nature, the group format can create conditions where an individual crisis gets masked — the facilitator should pay special attention to the following signs:

  • A member expressing a threat directed at another person or themselves within the group;
  • A member gradually withdrawing from the group, becoming silent (a sharp change compared to previous engagement);
  • Bullying or an insulting dynamic emerging within the group;
  • Risk factors that weren't apparent in individual sessions but surface under group pressure.
  1. If a threat is voiced within the group, immediately pull the session aside as facilitator and move to an individual conversation.
  2. If withdrawal is observed, do a brief individual check-in with that member after the group.
  3. If a bullying dynamic is detected, revisit the group rules, and reassess participant composition if needed.
  4. Keep a brief note on each member after every group session — group dynamics can hide individual risks.

Note: A group facilitator has to track 6-8 individual clinical pictures at the same time — without a structured note-taking system, subtle risk signals can easily be missed.

📌 Module summary

  • Group CBT is as effective as individual therapy, and additionally creates a sense of "universality."
  • The optimal group size is 5-8 people, and selecting from a similar problem/age group matters.
  • The facilitator directs questions to the whole group, not to an individual.
  • Difficult, sensitive topics are worked through in individual sessions, not in the group.
