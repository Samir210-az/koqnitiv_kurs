Module 17 · Family Transition

Working with Children of Divorcing Families

Two houses, two rooms, two different routines — for one child. This program is for supportive work with children whose families are going through divorce. It isn't about reconciling the parents — it's about helping the child feel safe through this transition.

1

Clinical experience: preventing loyalty conflict

Aysu was 9, and she'd behave in all sorts of contradictory ways to "prove her love" to each parent — speaking badly of her father around her mother, then doing the reverse around her father. This wasn't "lying." It was a loyalty conflict — she was splitting herself apart trying to stay loyal to both parents. That's an enormous weight for a child that age to carry.

The first step in working with this conflict is usually a separate conversation with the parents — I explain how important it is not to speak negatively about the other parent in front of the child. Then I work with the child directly — I tell her that loving both parents is entirely possible, that it isn't betrayal. Parents sometimes resist hearing this at first, because the urge to channel their own pain through the child is strong — usually unconscious, never malicious.

Note: What I told Aysu: "You don't have to choose anyone. Love doesn't split — it grows." Her mother told me later that she remembered that sentence for a long time.
2

Scientific definition: divorce in the context of Adjustment Disorder

Clinical definition: Divorce itself isn't a separate diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR, but as an acute stressor in children it can trigger Adjustment Disorder — behavioral/emotional symptoms arising within 3 months of the event. Research shows the strongest predictor of a child's outcome isn't the divorce itself, but the level of ongoing conflict between the parents (Amato's "Risk and Resilience" model).
In plain terms: Divorce can feel to a child like "the ground shifting" — everything familiar changes. But children very often blame themselves, when it's never their fault.
3

How it looks across age groups

👶 Younger children

  • Self-blame ("because I was naughty")
  • Fantasies that the parents will get back together

🧑 Teenagers

  • Anger, taking a side against one parent
  • Premature "growing up," taking on adult responsibility
4

A multidisciplinary view

🧠

Psychological view

Children very often blame themselves (egocentric thinking) — this has to be corrected clearly, repeatedly, without rushing.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family systems view

Loyalty conflict (pressure to "take sides" between parents) creates enormous stress for a child, sometimes showing up again years later.

🏫

Psychopedagogical view

Changes in attention and behavior at school can be linked to the divorce process — coordinating with the teacher helps.

5

Sample dialogue (with Aysu)

Aysu:Maybe if I'd been a better kid, my parents wouldn't have split up.
Therapist:Aysu, that wasn't your decision to make. It was something between the grown-ups.
Aysu:But I wanted to see them happy together.
Therapist:I understand — that's a really lovely thing to want. But you were never the cause of it.
Note: Saying this once isn't enough — children often need to hear this belief repeated across several sessions, in different forms, before they fully accept it.

7

For both parents

🚫 Don't make the child a "spy"

Asking about the other parent, or having the child carry messages, puts the child in the middle.

🗓️ Keep the routine stable

Similar rules and schedules across both homes give the child a sense of safety.

🤝 Stay respectful in front of the child

Even where you disagree, don't speak badly about the other parent in front of the child.

Homework: This week, each parent should set aside one-on-one time with the child — no discussion of the situation, just a shared activity. 20 minutes is enough. Consistency matters more than length.
8

Aysu's story: two homes, one heart

"When I'm with Mom I miss Dad, when I'm with Dad I miss Mom" — hearing that sentence from a child makes you fully understand what divorce means to a child.

📋 Presentation

Aysu was 7, and her parents had divorced 6 months earlier. Her teacher reported that she'd sometimes burst into tears out of nowhere, and would say things like "I'm a bad kid, that's why Dad left." Both parents were separately very worried, but weren't speaking to each other.

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that Aysu believed the divorce happened because of her — a very common "egocentric interpretation" at this age. The real work wasn't "fixing" the divorce, but clearly and repeatedly communicating to Aysu that she wasn't to blame.

Therapist:Aysu, you said your dad left because of you. How do you know that?
Client:I'd misbehaved before that, and then Mom and Dad argued a lot.
Therapist:I understand why you'd think that. But when grown-ups can't get along with each other, it's not because of a child's behavior — that's entirely something between the adults.
Client:Really? I didn't do anything wrong at all?
Therapist:You did nothing wrong at all. Both your dad and your mom love you — that will never change, even living in separate homes.
Core principle: Younger children very often attribute the divorce to themselves (egocentric thinking). The therapist's most important job is to question this belief clearly, again and again.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Aysu, I held an initial meeting separately with each parent — I had to prepare the parents myself first, so they could deliver a calm, united message in front of the child.

Assessment scales

📊

CBAPS (Children's Beliefs About Parental Divorce Scale)

A purpose-built tool that directly measures a child's self-blame and anxiety beliefs about the divorce.

📊

CPIC (Children's Perception of Interparental Conflict Scale)

Assesses how the child perceives conflict between the parents.

📊

SDQ (Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire)

Used alongside parent/teacher versions to track overall emotional and behavioral adjustment.

Note: Aysu's CBAPS result showed high self-blame at the start; after 4 sessions that score dropped substantially — both the therapeutic conversations and the parents' joint message played a decisive role.
12

Warning signs of risk in a child during divorce

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Parental divorce isn't a trauma in itself, but the dynamics that come with it can raise risk — the therapist should stay especially alert to these:

  • The child being drawn in as a "go-between" in the parents' conflict (carrying messages, being pressured to take sides);
  • Sharp alienation from one parent — a sudden break in what had been a warm relationship;
  • Deep guilt tied to the child seeing themselves as the "cause" of the divorce;
  • Sharp behavioral changes combined with financial or housing instability.
  1. If you find the child is being used as a messenger, discuss its impact on the child directly with both parents together.
  2. If signs of alienation are strong, suggest coordinating with a family law professional/mediator — this can fall outside the scope of therapeutic work.
  3. Normalize the guilt, but if it deepens, run an additional mood screening.
  4. If behavioral changes tied to financial instability intensify, refer to social support resources.

Note: Research shows that it isn't the divorce itself but the intensity of the ongoing parental conflict that's the main factor determining a child's adjustment.

📌 Module summary

  • Children very often interpret the divorce as their own fault — this needs to be corrected openly.
  • Protecting the child from loyalty conflict (pressure to take sides) matters for their wellbeing.
  • A stable routine and ongoing contact with both parents eases adjustment.
  • Cooperation between the parents significantly improves outcomes for the child.
