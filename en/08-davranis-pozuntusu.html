Module 08 · Behavior Work

Conduct Disorder

When I first started working with Tural, his teachers called him "unmanageable." Ten sessions later, that same teacher called me and asked, "What did you do to him?" My answer was simple — I didn't "do" anything to him, we just found the need behind his behavior. Below is a program built on functional assessment for working with conduct problems in children and teens. This is an approach built on understanding, not punishment.

1

Clinical definition: Conduct Disorder

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, conduct disorder is a repeated pattern of behavior that violates the basic rights of others or age-appropriate social norms: aggression toward people/animals, destruction of property, deceit/theft, serious rule violations. Per Ross Greene's Collaborative Problem Solving model, these behaviors are the result of missing regulation, flexibility, or problem-solving skills.
In plain terms: Conduct disorder means a child or teen persistently breaking rules and showing aggressive or destructive behaviors. But let's not forget: behavior is a form of communication. Very often the child is "showing" through behavior a need they can't manage or express any other way.

I repeat Ross Greene's famous line in every training I give: kids do well if they can. It's not "won't," it's usually "can't" — the difference may look small, but it completely changes the intervention strategy.

2

A multidisciplinary view

🧠

Psychological

Deficits in emotion regulation, learned behavior patterns — a behavior that once produced a desired result keeps getting repeated.

🏫

Educational psychology

Academic failure, a poorly-fitted teaching environment. Facing a hard task, a child sometimes chooses to "show they won't" instead of "show they can't."

🗣️

Speech therapist

Communication difficulty deepens frustration — a child who can't put a need into words shows it through behavior instead.

✋

Occupational therapist / sensory

Sensory seeking or overload can look like aggressive behavior — when the body feels dysregulated, behavior becomes dysregulated too.

3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children

  • Tantrums, pushing, throwing objects
  • Persistent resistance to rules

🧑 Teens

  • Lying, stealing, skipping class
  • Ongoing conflict with authority

🧑‍🦱 Adults (behavioral traces)

  • Rule-breaking at work, impulsivity
  • A persistent pattern of conflict in relationships
4

Practical technique: The ABC Behavior Model

I use this model in every first assessment. It's simple, but its power is exactly in that simplicity.

A

Antecedent

The trigger before the behavior

B

Behavior

The behavior itself

C

Consequence

What happens after the behavior

I ask the parent or teacher to track these three columns for a week: what happened before the behavior, what the behavior itself was, what happened after. After a few days, a pattern usually reveals itself — often the same trigger, the same outcome, repeating. The consequence column is especially interesting, because often the very "consequence" is what reinforces the behavior — for instance, if a child gets excused from a task by yelling, that accidentally rewards the behavior.

Important: Finding the "function" of the behavior (getting attention, escaping a hard task, seeking a sensation) is the key to the right intervention. Most of Tural's behavior, for example, was about escaping difficult math tasks — if we'd just punished it without finding that, nothing would have changed.
5

Sample dialogue (with Tural, searching for the function)

Therapist:Tural, what happened right before you flipped the table in class?
Teen:The teacher gave me a task, I couldn't do it.
Therapist:What did you feel in that moment?
Teen:I looked stupid in front of everyone, I got upset.
Therapist:So flipping the table was really about "getting away from this task, hiding the shame"?
Note: With teens, this kind of question-and-answer is done in a more analytical form — with children, I find the same function through drawing or play. Finding the function always comes first, the choice of technique comes after.

7

Guidance for parents and teachers

🏅 Notice good behavior

It's easy to react to bad behavior — noticing good behavior is what usually gets forgotten.

📐 Stable, clear rules

Children show less resistance in a predictable environment.

🤝 Home-school coordination

Different rules in different environments create confusion. Use the same language, the same expectations, as much as possible.

⏸️ Pause before reacting

In the moment of an outburst, instead of reacting right away, take a breath yourself too — your own calm is a model for the child.

Homework: This week, have the parent/teacher keep an ABC chart — three columns: trigger, behavior, consequence. We'll look at the pattern together at the next session. These notes often become the most valuable material in the whole therapy process.
➕

Tural: not a "difficult kid," a "missing skill"

Ross Greene's idea is the leitmotif of this module: kids do well if they can. My work with Tural was really about testing this exact principle.

📋 Presentation

Tural was 10 years old, and would yell and sometimes hit his desk when a task in class got hard. The teacher called him "manipulative, attention-seeking." But once the ABC observation sheet was filled out, the pattern became clear: the outbursts always happened right before "hard, not-quickly-solvable" tasks, not after getting attention.

🔍 What we found

The functional assessment showed that the function of Tural's behavior was "escape" — escaping difficulty, escaping the feeling of failure. Not attention — that was the real motive. This completely changed the treatment plan: instead of reducing attention, the task itself needed to be made manageable.

Therapist:Tural, right before you hit the desk, what were you feeling about the task?
Client:I knew I wouldn't be able to do it, I thought everyone would laugh.
Therapist:So yelling was really saying "I need help"?
Client:Maybe... but that's not how I say it.
Therapist:Exactly right, because you haven't learned the way to say it yet. That's exactly what we'll work on today — how to say it in words.
Key observation: Tural's behavior wasn't "bad intent" — it was a skills deficit (problem-solving, frustration tolerance). This reframe also changed how the teacher approached him.

🎯 How I apply this in session

We gave Tural a "I need help" card — he could raise it wordlessly when things got hard. This small tool brought his outbursts down from 5 a week to 1, within the first month.

Therapeutic Assessment Form

In conduct disorders, the formulation is built on functional analysis — no intervention works without finding "why" the behavior serves its purpose.

ComponentTural's case
Presenting problemYelling in class, episodes of hitting objects
Precipitating factorsDifficult academic tasks, moments where failure feels imminent
Predisposing factorsLow frustration tolerance, a history of early learning difficulties
Perpetuating factorsThe "escape" function of the behavior working effectively, the teacher's punishment-based reaction
Protective factorsGood verbal skill potential, a parent open to cooperation
Core belief"I won't be able to do it, so it's better not to try"
Treatment goalAlternative communication skills, breaking the task into manageable parts

🎯 How I apply this in session

I also shared this table with the teacher (with the parent's permission) — the "perpetuating factors" row showed the teacher how their own reaction needed to change.

CBT assessment tests and scales

I use these tools for conduct disorders, usually gathering parallel information from the parent and the teacher.

📊

NCBRF (Nisonger Child Behavior Rating Form)

A broad tool assessing behavior problems and social skills in parallel.

📊

DBRS (Disruptive Behavior Rating Scale)

Measures ODD and conduct disorder symptoms per DSM-5 criteria.

📊

FAST (Functional Assessment Screening Tool)

A structured tool that helps identify the function of behavior (attention, escape, stimulation, access).

📊

Vanderbilt Behavior Scale

Measures behavior and attention symptoms in parallel, with teacher and parent versions.

📊

SDQ (Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire)

A rapid screening tool covering both difficulties and strengths.

Note: Tural's FAST result clearly pointed to the "escape" function — confirming the intervention needed to start with the difficulty of the task, not attention.
🏠

Sample homework

Here's the specific assignment I gave Tural.

This week, when a task gets hard, raise the "I need help" card instead of yelling or throwing something. Every time you do this, draw a star in your notebook — we're not tracking how many times you yelled, but how many times you used the card. The goal isn't to be perfect, it's to try the new way just a little more often.

8

Escalation indicators in conduct disorder

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Resistance and escalation are common in a conduct disorder program, but the following signs go beyond the scope of this program and require urgent intervention:

  • Law-breaking behaviors (theft, violence) increasing in frequency and severity;
  • Episodes of cruelty to animals or fire-setting — a serious prognostic indicator of Conduct Disorder;
  • Family violence shifting into a mutual direction (physical threat from child toward parent);
  • Emerging signs of substance use, especially in teens.
  1. Track the frequency and severity of law-breaking behaviors in a structured way at every session.
  2. If an episode of animal cruelty or fire-setting is identified, make an urgent referral for a full psychiatric evaluation.
  3. If mutual family violence is identified, build a separate safety plan for the family and coordinate with the relevant agency.
  4. If substance use is suspected, refer for specialized assessment — that falls outside the scope of this program.

Note: Parent Management Training is the core of this program, but on its own it isn't enough in cases with serious conduct disorder signs — a multidisciplinary team is required.
