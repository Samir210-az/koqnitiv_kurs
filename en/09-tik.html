Module 09 · Tic Disorders

Tic Program

Orxan's mother cried on our first call — "the teacher sent him out of class, everyone was staring at him." Orxan was 9 years old, and his eye blinking repeated hundreds of times a day. Eight sessions later, the tic was still there, but it no longer ran Orxan's life. Below is a program based on Habit Reversal Training (HRT) for working with tic disorders in children and teens. In this program, patience — for both the therapist and the family — is the single most important requirement.

1

Clinical definition: Tic Disorders

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, tics are sudden, rapid, recurring, non-rhythmic motor movements or vocalizations. If multiple motor tics plus at least 1 vocal tic persist for more than 1 year, a Tourette Syndrome diagnosis may apply. The gold-standard behavioral treatment is the HRT (Habit Reversal Training) and CBIT (Comprehensive Behavioral Intervention for Tics) protocol.
In plain terms: A tic is a sudden, repetitive, involuntary movement (a motor tic: eye blinking, shoulder shrugging) or sound (a vocal tic: throat clearing). Often, right before the tic, there's a feeling of "pressure" or "itch" in the body (a premonitory urge) — this feeling is the tic's "early signal."

I tell parents in the very first minute — a tic isn't a matter of willpower. Saying "hold it in" is like asking someone to suppress a cough. It's possible for a while, but it comes out stronger in the end. Our goal isn't to "eliminate" the tic, it's to teach how to manage it.

2

A multidisciplinary view

🧠 Psychological perspective

Stress and anxiety increase the severity of a tic, but aren't the cause — tics are neurologically based. Explaining this to the client often lifts a big weight, because self-blame decreases.

✋ Occupational therapist / sensory perspective

The premonitory urge is really a sensory feeling — the body seems to "demand" a certain movement. Sensory regulation skills can help manage this feeling.

🗣️ Speech therapist perspective

Vocal tics (throat clearing, making sounds) can affect the flow of speech — a speech therapist supports breath-voice coordination in this situation.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

Attention being drawn to a tic in class affects a child's self-confidence — coordinating with the teacher is important.

3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children

  • Little awareness of the tic, need for a simple explanation
  • Sensitivity to a parent's anxiety

🧑 Teens

  • Social shame, attempts to hide it
  • Performance pressure that causes the tic to intensify
4

The core method: Habit Reversal Training (HRT)

Habit Reversal Training (HRT) is the behavioral treatment with the strongest evidence base for tics. It has two core components:

🔍

Awareness training

Recognizing the pre-tic body feeling early.

🔄

Competing response

An alternative movement, physically opposite to the tic, that draws little attention.

Here's the underlying logic. First, we find the "early signal" together with the client — what feeling comes right before the shoulder shrug? Then, in response to this feeling, we teach a movement that's physically opposite to the tic and draws little attention — for instance, against a shoulder tic, gently holding the shoulders down and relaxed. This new movement is applied the moment the tic signal is felt. Experience shows that in the first weeks, this feels hard for the client — because the new movement hasn't become automatic yet. But gradually, especially with daily practice, the competing response becomes automatic too.

Note: The competing response must be the exact opposite of the tic, but must draw no attention in a social setting. The "shoulder-freedom" technique we used with Orxan wasn't noticed by his friends at all — that made a huge difference for him.
5

Sample dialogue (with Orxan, awareness practice)

Therapist:Orxan, what do you feel in your body right before you blink?
Child:I don't know... something like an itch, a little.
Therapist:Great observation! That's the "early signal" of your tic. Now, the moment you catch that feeling, try the movement I showed you.
Child:(tries) That was a bit helpful.
Note: With children, I present the awareness practice as a game — like being a "detective" investigating your own body. With teens, I explain it more directly, in analytical language.

7

Guidance for family and teachers

🚫 Don't say "stop it"

This increases the pressure the tic creates, producing a stronger tic in the long run.

👀 Don't draw attention to it

Directing attention to the tic (even out of concern) negatively affects the child's self-confidence.

🏫 Stay in touch with the teacher

Without any unnecessary explanation to the class, simply a considerate attitude is enough.

Homework: This week, have the client carefully observe themselves once a day to recognize the pre-tic "early signal" — without trying to intervene, just noticing it and noting it down.
8

Orxan's story: from blinking to peace

Sometimes a family comes to me saying "we've already tried everything." Orxan's parents were like that too — they'd taken him to three different doctors for his blinking, and all of them had said "it'll pass." It hadn't.

📋 Presentation

Orxan was 10 years old, and had been living with eye blinking and throat clearing for 8 months. At school, some kids laughed at him, asking "why do you do that," and he grew more and more withdrawn. His mother told me at our first meeting: "When I tell him to 'stop it,' it gets a little better, but then it gets worse." That sentence already told me a lot.

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that the tics intensified during moments of fatigue and tension (class, evening hours), and almost disappeared during calm play. This is a classic tic pattern. But the real problem wasn't the tic itself — it was how the family reacted to it. The demand to "stop it" reduced the tic under short-term pressure, then it came back stronger with a "rebound" effect.

Therapist:Orxan, what do you feel when your mom says "stop it"?
Client:I feel embarrassed. Then I try to hold it in more, but it gets even worse.
Therapist:Let's unpack that a bit — what happens in your body when you try to hold it in?
Client:It's like I get even more tense, and then it comes out really strong all at once.
Therapist:That's exactly right. Forcing a tic to hold in is like keeping pressurized steam in one place — the harder you squeeze, the stronger it comes out somewhere.
Client:So holding it in doesn't help?
Therapist:Not in the long run. That's why, instead of "holding it in," we'll learn a different way — the competing response. It doesn't suppress the tic, it just redirects the energy elsewhere.
Core principle: Applying force as a strategy against a tic almost always backfires. Explaining this to the family is nearly as important to treatment success as the awareness training itself.

🎯 How I apply this in session

When working with Orxan, I devoted the first three sessions entirely to trust-building and psychoeducation, without touching the technique at all. HRT started too early often fails, because the client still sees their own body as an "enemy."

Assessment scales for tic disorders

For tic disorders, unlike depression/anxiety scales, I use tic-specific tools — these measure severity and functional impact.

📊

YGTSS (Yale Global Tic Severity Scale)

The gold-standard tool measuring the number, frequency, intensity, and functional impact of motor and vocal tics.

📊

PUTS (Premonitory Urge for Tics Scale)

Measures the intensity of the pre-tic "early signal" feeling — useful for tracking HRT's effectiveness.

📊

CGI-S (Clinical Global Impression-Severity)

A brief, rapid tool for the clinician's overall severity assessment.

📊

GAD-7

Since tics are related to stress, I track overall anxiety levels in parallel.

Note: I applied the YGTSS with Orxan at the start of treatment and after the 8th session — the frequency of blinking had dropped by 40%, but more importantly, the functional-impact score had dropped to nearly zero: he no longer felt uneasy in class.
🏠

Sample homework

Here's the specific assignment I gave Orxan.

During the day, write down in your journal the moments when you were able to catch the "early signal" right before a tic — when, where, what you felt. In the moments you caught it, try the competing response we learned, but don't be hard on yourself if it doesn't work. The goal isn't perfection, it's practice. I tell every family: this process isn't linear, it'll go forward sometimes and backward other times — that's normal.

12

The limits of assessment in a tic program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

The CBIT (Comprehensive Behavioral Intervention for Tics) model is a functional behavioral program, but the following signs indicate the program's limits have been exceeded:

  • Tics reaching a level of self-harm (hard hitting, skin-injuring movements);
  • Deepening social isolation — the child refusing to go to school entirely;
  • Comorbid OCD or ADHD symptoms worsening to a degree that impedes the tic program's effectiveness;
  • A sharp worsening of mood due to bullying at home/school.
  1. If a tic reaches a self-harm level, refer for a medical (neurological) evaluation alongside CBIT.
  2. If bullying is identified, coordinate with the school to build a safety plan.
  3. If comorbid OCD/ADHD symptoms are impeding the program, decide on prioritization together with the family.
  4. Add a structured screening (PHQ-9/SDQ) related to the mood worsening.

Note: Tic severity and functional impairment don't always overlap — the main criterion in assessment is how much the child's daily functioning is limited.

📌 Module summary

  • A tic is an involuntary, neurologically based movement or sound, not a weakness of will.
  • HRT (awareness + competing response) is the most effective treatment method.
  • The premonitory urge — the early body signal — is the key point of intervention.
  • The goal isn't to eliminate the tic, it's to build a healthy managing relationship with it.
