Module 19 · Speech Fluency

Stuttering (Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder) Program

Kamal's mother told me one sentence on her first call: "He doesn't raise his hand in class anymore, it's like he's afraid to speak." But at home, eating chips, joking around with his father, his speech flowed completely freely. That difference told me everything — the problem wasn't in the words, it was in the situations. This is a multidisciplinary program that brings together speech therapy, psychology, and sensory integration perspectives for working with stuttering.

1

Scientific definition: Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder

In the DSM-5-TR, stuttering is classified under "Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder" (315.35 / F80.81) — a disruption in the normal fluency and timing of speech, characterized by repetitions, prolongations, and blocks of sounds and syllables.

In his classic work, Barry Guitar describes stuttering not merely as a motor speech problem, but as an interaction between the physiological, cognitive, and emotional layers of speech production. Anne Smith and Ellen Kelly's Multifactorial Dynamic Pathways model describes stuttering not as a fixed state, but as a dynamic process that shifts day to day, even sentence to sentence. I explain it to families this way: it's like driving a car on a road that's sometimes smooth, sometimes bumpy — the car itself isn't broken, the road conditions are what change.

The iceberg model (Joseph Sheehan): Above the waterline are the visible parts — repetitions, prolongations, blocks. Below the waterline is a larger, invisible part — fear, shame, avoidance of words and situations. Therapy has to work not just the visible part, but what's under the water too.
2

A multidisciplinary view

🗣️

Speech-language pathologist

Teaches fluency techniques directly (easy onset, slow speech, stuttering modification), tracks coordination of the speech organs.

🧠

Clinical psychologist

Works on reducing shame, social anxiety, and avoidance behaviors, and building self-confidence.

✋

Occupational therapist

Helps regulate breath-movement coordination and overall body tension — the speech organs are part of the body too.

🏫

Psychopedagogue

Adapts the school environment, coordinates with the teacher, and helps build a supportive classroom setting.

🎯

Sensory integration specialist

Builds calming strategies to reduce the impact of high arousal states (excitement, rushing) on speech fluency.

3

How it looks across age groups

👶

Early childhood (ages 2-6)

At this age, most stuttering resolves naturally. According to Ehud Yairi's research, fluency returns even without intervention in a significant portion of this group, though an early assessment is always recommended.

🎒

School age

Social awareness increases, and classmates' reactions start to matter. Avoidance behaviors (word substitution, not raising a hand) begin to form at this age.

🧑

Teens and adults

The long-term emotional burden, social anxiety, and the risk of self-limiting in career and relationships all increase. Therapy in this group leans more heavily toward the emotional component.

4

Clinical case: Kamal's story

📋 Presentation

Kamal was 9, and his stuttering had been present since age 4, but had become more noticeable after he started school. He wouldn't raise his hand in class, and would refer to a friend as "that boy" because he couldn't say his name. At home, with his family, he spoke comfortably — that difference told me the problem was contextual.

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that Kamal's stuttering intensified in situations with performance pressure (answering questions in class, speaking under time pressure), and nearly disappeared in relaxed, pressure-free contexts. This is a classic "situation-dependent" pattern of stuttering. The core work was to address both the speech mechanics and the performance fear in parallel.

Core principle: Stuttering isn't a fixed "disorder" — it's a dynamic process that shifts from day to day, situation to situation. Treatment needs to address both the speech mechanics and the emotional component.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Kamal, I always start the work with breath and easy onset, then gradually move to the emotional component — the body needs to relax first, then the mind.

5

Sample dialogue (with Kamal)

Therapist:Kamal, why do you hold back from raising your hand in class?
Client:I'm afraid I'll get stuck, and everyone will laugh.
Therapist:That fear makes a lot of sense. So why is talking comfortable at home?
Client:Nobody's watching me there.
Therapist:So the problem isn't in your voice — it shows up wherever attention and pressure build up. That's exactly what we're going to ease together.
Clinical observation: The moment you show a family this contextual variability, the shift from "fix him" to "let's make this easier for him" often happens right then.
6

Practical technique: Easy Onset and Diaphragmatic Breathing

The Easy Onset technique teaches speech to start gently and gradually rather than sharply — this reduces the tension across the vocal folds.

Steps: (1) Take a deep breath from the belly. (2) As you exhale, start the sound very softly, almost like a whisper. (3) Gradually bring the word up to normal volume. This technique is practiced first with single vowels (a, o, u), then single words, then short sentences.

7

Practical technique: Stuttering Modification (Van Riper)

The goal of this approach isn't to eliminate stuttering entirely, but to turn it into a lighter, less tense form.

1

Cancellation

After a moment of stuttering ends, you pause, then say the word again, slowly and comfortably.

2

Pull-out

While the stutter is still happening, you slowly and deliberately pull out of it — without stopping.

3

Preparatory set

For a word you can sense in advance will be hard, you prepare with an easy onset before you reach it.

Assessment scales

📊

SSI-4 (Stuttering Severity Instrument)

The gold-standard clinical tool measuring the frequency, duration, and accompanying physical tension of stuttering.

📊

OASES (Overall Assessment of the Speaker's Experience of Stuttering)

A broad tool measuring stuttering's functional impact on daily life, communication, and emotional state.

📊

KiddyCAT / CAT (Communication Attitude Test)

An age-appropriate tool measuring a child's attitude toward their own speech and their speech-related anxiety.

Note: Kamal's SSI-4 result was in the moderate severity range, but his OASES result showed that the functional impact (social avoidance) was a bigger problem than the severity itself — this led treatment to lean heavily into the emotional component.
12

Recommendations for parents and teachers

⏳ Don't rush them

Instead of saying "speak slowly," model it by speaking slowly yourself — a child gets stuck more in a rushed environment.

👁️ Keep eye contact, don't finish the word

Wait patiently for the child to finish what they're saying, instead of finishing it for them.

🚫 Don't say "stop, say it again"

This demand actually increases tension and reinforces self-criticism.

🤝 Stay in touch with the school

Keeping the teacher informed helps build a supportive classroom environment.

🏠

Sample homework assignment

What I gave Kamal: 5 minutes a day of diaphragmatic breathing and easy onset practice with the family, trying three or four sentences in the slow style. At least once this week, knowing it will be hard, say a word without avoiding it. Note the attempt regardless of outcome — the goal isn't perfection, it's courage.

📌 Module summary

  • Stuttering isn't fixed — it's a dynamic process that shifts from situation to situation.
  • The iceberg model helps distinguish the visible part (repetition, prolongation) from the invisible part (shame, avoidance).
  • Speech techniques (easy onset, slow speech, stuttering modification) address the physiological component.
  • The emotional component — shame and avoidance — matters just as much and needs to be addressed in parallel.
  • A multidisciplinary team approach (speech therapist, psychologist, occupational therapist, psychopedagogue) is the most effective.

Psychosocial risk signs in the stuttering program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

Stuttering itself doesn't carry psychiatric risk, but the social experience that comes with it can lead to the following signs, which shouldn't be overlooked:

  • Complete refusal to speak due to bullying (a shift toward mutism);
  • Deepening social isolation, avoidance of going to school;
  • Shame about speech having a negative effect on overall self-esteem, turning into expressions of hopelessness;
  • A parent's excessive correcting behavior creating additional anxiety in the child.
  1. If signs of bullying are found, contact the school and build a support plan.
  2. If complete refusal to speak continues for more than 2 weeks, refer for an additional psychological assessment.
  3. If expressions of hopelessness intensify, add a mood screening.
  4. Work with the parent on patient-listening strategies in place of correcting behavior.

Note: The success of a stuttering program isn't measured by speech fluency alone, but by whether the child stops being afraid to speak — the psychosocial component matters as much as the speech-language component.
