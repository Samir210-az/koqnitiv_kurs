Module 02 · Working with Trauma

Post-Traumatic Stress and CBT

I walk into this module a little differently every time, if I'm honest. When trauma enters the room, you can't rush — not in what you say, not in the pace you set. Below is a 12-session road map shaped by years of practice: real dialogues, workbook materials, and a pace that adapts from young children all the way to adults. Read it, but don't rush — this module itself is really a lesson in not rushing.

1

Scientific definition: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

Clinical definition: According to the DSM-5-TR, PTSD is characterized by four symptom clusters: re-experiencing, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity. These emerge after directly experiencing, witnessing, or learning that a close person experienced actual or threatened death or serious injury, and persist for at least one month. TF-CBT is currently the protocol with the strongest evidence base for children and adolescents.
In plain terms: Picture the smoke alarm in your house. Now imagine that same alarm going off when you're just toasting bread, or even when it sees a bit of steam. The body has this system to protect you — but after trauma it shifts into "hyper-alert" mode, and keeps reacting to a danger that's long past as though it's happening right now. This is exactly how I explain it to a client.

I always see relief on someone's face the first time they hear this. Because it isn't a failure of willpower. It's a survival mechanism that did its job, that the body simply hasn't learned to switch off yet — it's still fighting an old battle.

2

Clinical classification of symptoms (DSM-5)

When making a diagnosis I look at four symptom clusters, all of which must last more than a month and meaningfully disrupt daily life. What follows isn't just a list — each item carries a real clinical picture behind it.

🔄

1. Re-experiencing (intrusion)

The event unwillingly "returns" — in the form of flashbacks, nightmares, sudden distressing memories.

Röya, age 8, "saw" the moment of her accident again every time a car horn sounded afterward. As if time had rewound.
🚪

2. Avoidance

Any place, person, or topic that serves as a reminder is avoided by every possible means.

A teenager who survived a car accident no longer wanted to take that road — not even as a passenger.
☁️

3. Negative shifts in thought and mood

Negative beliefs form about oneself, others, and the world. Emotional numbness and withdrawal from joy belong here too.

"The world is dangerous." "You can't trust anyone." I hear these sentences constantly.
⚡

4. Marked hyperarousal

Constant anticipation of danger, being easily startled, sleep trouble, irritability, difficulty concentrating.

Flinching at the smallest sound. The body never seems to fully settle.
3

How presentation differs by age

The same trauma shows up in completely different forms in a child, a teenager, an adult. This is the first thing I check when choosing a program — how does age dress up the symptom?

👶 Children (ages 3–10)

  • Re-enacting the trauma repeatedly through play
  • Regression (returning to younger behaviors, thumb-sucking)
  • Separation anxiety, sleep problems
  • Somatic complaints (stomachaches, headaches)

🧑 Adolescents (ages 11–17)

  • Risk-taking behavior, aggression
  • Social withdrawal, avoiding school
  • Risk of self-harm
  • Intensified shame and guilt

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Emotional numbing, withdrawal from relationships
  • Decline in work and daily functioning
  • Sleep disturbances, ongoing tension
  • Self-criticism, reduced self-confidence

In young children, the body's reaction arrives before words do. That's why I always bring a sensory-integration lens into this work — weighted blankets, movement-based tools like dance or tapping, are enormously useful. From an occupational-therapy point of view: the body has to settle first before the words can come.

4

The TF-CBT model — the PRACTICE components

Trauma-Focused CBT is the most extensively researched, evidence-based trauma treatment model in the world. The acronym "PRACTICE" helps hold the core steps in mind — I still sometimes plan a session by running through this word myself.

P

Psychoeducation and parenting skills

I explain trauma and its effects in plain language, and give the parent concrete support skills.

R

Relaxation skills

Breathing techniques, progressive muscle relaxation — concrete tools that calm the body.

A

Affect regulation

Recognizing a feeling, naming it, learning to manage it.

C

Cognitive coping

Showing the thought-feeling-behavior link, gently questioning unhelpful thoughts.

T

Trauma narrative

Putting the event into words, step by step, in a safe environment. This is the most sensitive stage of all.

I

In-vivo exposure

We build gradual contact with real, safe, but avoided situations.

C

Conjoint parent-child sessions

Sharing the child's story with the parent, strengthening the family bond.

E

Enhancing safety

We build a concrete, written safety plan for the future.

6

Sample dialogue (with an 8-year-old)

Therapist:Röya, there's a little "danger bell" in our brain. It's there to protect us. But sometimes, after something scary happens, that bell gets extra sensitive and rings even when it doesn't need to. What's your bell like?
Child:My bell rings all the time! Even when I'm sleeping.
Therapist:I hear you, that must be really exhausting. Shall we learn together how to calm that bell down a little?
Child:Yes, I want the bell to be a bit quieter.
Note: With children, metaphor, play, and drawing are the main tools. I never use the word "PTSD" with a child. I speak in images they can actually grasp.

Workbook material: Box Breathing

This is one of the tools that never leaves my bag. Breathe in as the circle grows, breathe out as it shrinks. That simple — but the effect is anything but simple.

10

Guidance for parents

🗣️ Don't force them to talk

The child will talk when they're ready. Give it time, don't shower them with questions.

🛏️ Protect routine

Regular sleep, meals, daily activity — this does more work than words. This is exactly how a sense of safety reaches a child.

💬 Validate the feeling

Instead of saying "don't be scared," say "I understand you're scared, I'm right here with you."

🚫 Avoid re-traumatizing

Avoid asking about the event's details over and over again.

🏫 Stay in touch with school

Let the teacher know what's going on so the child is supported at school too. This is the simplest form of psycho-educational support.

Homework (for the parent): Every evening this week, spend five minutes with your child on a "how was your day" chat — no barrage of questions, just listen. Jot down one sentence: what was different today?
11

Working with Röya: what's behind the story

Sometimes you look into a child's eyes and think you've already understood everything. That was my first thought with Röya too — then I realized I was wrong.

📋 Presentation

Röya was 9, brought to me by her mother three weeks after a car accident. She herself hadn't been seriously injured in the crash, but her mother had briefly lost consciousness, the car door was crushed, and the two of them had waited a long time on the roadside for help. Physically both had recovered. But Röya could no longer sleep at night, froze at every car horn, and had withdrawn from her classmates at school.

🔍 What we found

I didn't apply any formal test in the first three sessions — I simply built trust through play and drawing. In the fourth session, Röya herself, unprompted, began talking about the car. That was the key moment for me: when a child is ready, they open the door themselves — you shouldn't be knocking on it.

Therapist:Röya, do you want to talk to me today about that day, or is it still too soon?
Client:I don't know... but every time I hear a car horn, my heart beats really hard.
Therapist:That's a very understandable reaction. Your brain is trying to tell you "pay attention." What do you usually do when that feeling comes?
Client:I cover my ears, sometimes I move away from the window.
Therapist:I understand. I want to tell you something — remembering that day isn't dangerous by itself. The memory can't actually hurt you, it just makes you feel uneasy. But every time you run from it, your brain learns "so this really must be dangerous."
Client:So running away from it makes me more scared?
Therapist:Exactly right. Here, in small steps, we're going to learn to get closer to this memory — without rushing, but without running either.
Core principle: Remembering trauma isn't dangerous in itself. The main factor that keeps the problem going is systematic avoidance of trauma memories. I explain this to every family from the first session, because otherwise a parent can end up reinforcing avoidance in the name of protecting the child.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I never told Röya "don't be scared." Instead, we unpacked the logic of the fear together — why it exists, what purpose it serves, when it's no longer needed. Once a child understands that, the road ahead looks a little less frightening.

Case Formulation Worksheet

Since I'm working with a child, I usually fill this form out together with the parent — while noting separately the parts that come from the child's own words. On paper it looks simple. In practice, it's the skeleton of the entire treatment plan.

Presenting problemSleep disturbance, hyperarousal, social withdrawal
Precipitating factors (triggers)Car accident (3 weeks earlier)
Predisposing factors (roots)Sensitive temperament, prior history of separation anxiety
Perpetuating factors (why it doesn't stop)Systematic avoidance (car horns, windows, the road), family reinforcing the avoidance too
Protective factors (strengths)Supportive mother, good verbal expression, prior secure attachment
Core belief"The world is suddenly dangerous, I can't protect myself"

🎯 How I apply this in session

The "perpetuating factors" row in this table is the most important one for me — because treatment mostly begins right there. For Röya it was avoidance; for someone else it could be an entirely different pattern.

CBT assessment tools and scales

For children and adolescents with suspected PTSD, I use standardized tools before the first session. These don't produce a diagnosis, but they put a number on the starting point and let us track progress across treatment.

📊

CPSS-5 (Child PTSD Symptom Scale)

The primary tool for measuring PTSD symptom severity and functional impact in ages 8–18.

📊

UCLA PTSD Reaction Index

An international standard for assessing traumatic exposure and symptoms across a broad age range.

📊

CRIES-8

A brief scale measuring re-experiencing and avoidance symptoms, quick to apply in clinical practice.

📊

IES-R (Impact of Event Scale)

Assesses the impact of an event across three dimensions (re-experiencing, avoidance, arousal) in adolescents and adults.

📊

PCL-5

A self-report checklist of PTSD symptoms aligned with DSM-5 criteria, widely used with adults.

📊

TSCC (Trauma Symptom Checklist for Children)

An expanded tool covering broader post-trauma symptomatology, including anxiety, depression, and dissociation.

Note: With Röya I used the CPSS-5 and CRIES-8 together, repeating them every 4 sessions. A drop in the numbers often lands with a parent more convincingly than words do — "look, it was 7, now it's 5" creates real hope.
🏠

Homework example

This was the concrete assignment I gave Röya — it can serve as a template for you too.

Every day, at any point, instead of freezing when you hear a car horn, take three deep breaths and say to yourself out loud (or silently): "This is happening now, not in the past. Remembering the memory doesn't hurt me." Log it in your notebook with a number (fear level 0–10). The goal isn't to erase the fear instantly — it's simply to learn to stay with it without running. I tell every client this: the monster grows the more you run, and shrinks the more you stay.

15

Crisis signs during the post-trauma period

⚠️ When urgent intervention is required

Within the TF-CBT framework — especially once moving into the trauma narrative component — the following signs should immediately change the direction of the session:

  • Deepening dissociative episodes — a child losing contact with reality for an extended period (more than a few minutes);
  • Observed self-harming behavior during a flashback (hitting themselves, scratching);
  • Statements like "I don't want to live anymore," especially when approaching the hardest part of the trauma narrative;
  • A current environment carrying risk of re-traumatization (ongoing violence, an unsafe home).
  1. Pause the trauma narrative component; stabilize first with grounding (5-4-3-2-1) and breathing techniques.
  2. If dissociation is prolonged or recurring, arrange psychiatric consultation together with the parent.
  3. If a current safety risk (ongoing violence) is uncovered, the duty to report to the relevant child protection authority is triggered.
  4. For statements of self-harm, conduct a structured inquiry based on the Columbia Risk Scale and document the outcome.
  5. Before resuming, make sure the client has stabilized — rushing back into the narrative component can backfire.

Note: The "stabilization" phase in TF-CBT's PRACTICE model is a mandatory gate — rushing a client who isn't ready into the narrative component is a clinical error.

📌 Module summary

  • PTSD is the brain's danger-alarm system becoming oversensitized after trauma. It is not a failure of willpower.
  • There are four core symptom clusters: re-experiencing, avoidance, negative thought/mood shifts, marked hyperarousal.
  • Presentation differs by age group — the program needs to be adapted accordingly.
  • TF-CBT (the PRACTICE model) is the most evidence-based trauma treatment approach in the world.
  • Breathing techniques, the Fear Thermometer, grounding skills — tools within reach at every session.
  • Work with a child falls short without parent support. I've seen this play out for years.
