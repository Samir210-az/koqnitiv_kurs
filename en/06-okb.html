Module 06 · Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

OCD & CBT

When Kənan first came to me, he was washing his hands 40-50 times a day. His mother said the morning routine took two hours. Twelve sessions later, that number had dropped to 5-8 — close to a normal range. Below is the full ERP-based program I use, from children through adults. This program, honestly, is one of the ones that demands the most patience from me.

1

Clinical definition: Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, OCD is characterized by the presence of unwanted, recurring thoughts, urges, or images (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to neutralize them (compulsions). This process consumes more than 1 hour a day, or causes significant distress or functional impairment. The gold-standard treatment is the ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) protocol.
In plain terms: OCD is a state where the brain sends a "false alarm." Recurring, distressing thoughts arise. Certain actions are performed to "neutralize" them — handwashing, checking, counting. The problem is that the compulsion only relieves the anxiety temporarily. The thought comes back. The cycle continues.

When I explained this to Kənan, I told him: "You're not broken — your brain is just sending a false alarm signal, and you feel compelled to respond to it every time." I still remember the relief I saw on his face.

2

The OCD cycle

It's impossible to apply ERP without understanding this cycle. I always draw it out for the client first.

1

Obsession

A recurring, distressing thought

2

Anxiety

Intense discomfort arises

3

Compulsion

An action that brings temporary relief

Core intervention: ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) — being exposed to the obsession and waiting without performing the compulsion demonstrates that anxiety decreases on its own (habituation). Without the compulsion, anxiety usually comes down on its own within 20-40 minutes — this is the body's natural process, and most clients don't believe it the first time they hear it.
3

How it looks different across age groups

👶 Children (5-10 yrs)

  • Counting, ordering, demanding symmetry
  • Constantly seeking a parent's reassurance
  • Trying to pull the family into the ritual

🧑 Teens

  • Hiding symptoms, shame
  • Moral/religious obsessions (scrupulosity)
  • Impact on academic performance

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Functional limitations at work and in relationships
  • Symptoms hidden for years
  • Risk of co-occurring depression
4

A multidisciplinary view

🧠 Psychological perspective

"Thought-action fusion" — equating a thought with an action — is the central cognitive distortion in OCD. The client falls into the false belief that "if I thought it, I must want it."

✋ Occupational therapist / sensory perspective

In children seeking symmetry and a "just-right feeling," compulsions sometimes get mixed up with a sensory-regulation need — telling the two apart matters diagnostically.

🗣️ Speech therapist perspective

In young children, rituals sometimes become the only "language" for anxiety that can't be put into words — here we first teach naming the feeling.

🏫 Educational psychology perspective

When rituals eat into school time (like repeatedly straightening a notebook), it affects academic performance, and coordinating with the teacher becomes necessary.

5

Practical technique: ERP step by step

When applying ERP I always go through three phases. First, I build a hierarchy together with the client — from the easiest situation to the hardest, with a thermometer rating for each. Second, starting from the easiest rung, we get exposed to the obsession but don't allow the compulsion. Third, we watch together as the anxiety comes down on its own — this is the most convincing moment for the client, because they see it through their own experience, not just my words.

Note: ERP must always be conducted under professional supervision. Early in my career I once moved too fast on a step, and the client pulled back — since then I take every step only with the client's own consent, in small increments. Rushing always backfires here.

Sample dialogue (with Kənan, preparing for ERP)

Therapist:Kənan, while you wait without washing your hands, look at this graph — anxiety rises, then comes down on its own.
Teen:Really? I thought it would stay forever.
Therapist:No, this is the body's natural process. No emotion ever stays at the same level forever.
Teen:Okay, I want to try this.
Note: Showing this graph is more convincing than words — the client sees the number with their own eyes, and trusts their own experience rather than just my word.
8

Guidance for the family

🚫 Don't offer reassurance like "it's clean"

Even though it brings short-term relief, it reinforces the compulsion.

💬 Accept the feeling, not the ritual

"This must feel hard, I'm right here" — offer support without validating the ritual.

🤝 Stay in contact with the therapist

The family's "accommodation behavior" (making the ritual easier) can slow down progress.

Homework: This week, have the client simply count and note the frequency of handwashing/checking, without trying to change it — this is the first step itself: gathering data.
➕

Kənan and washing his hands 47 times

The first time people hear the number, it usually sounds unusual to most. But in OCD, the ritual never feels "too much" to the client themselves — it never feels "enough."

📋 Presentation

Kənan was 12 years old, washing his hands an average of 40-50 times a day, his skin was raw. Touching a door handle, sitting at his school desk, would trigger the thought "germs will spread, someone will get sick, it'll be my fault." The ritual calmed this thought temporarily, but it would start again within minutes.

🔍 What we found

The Y-BOCS-type assessment revealed that Kənan's obsession was "inflated responsibility" — as if his entire family's health rested in his hands. This is a very common cognitive distortion in childhood OCD.

Therapist:Kənan, if you washed your hands one less time today, what do you think would happen?
Client:Someone would get sick, because of me.
Therapist:What percentage do you think that thought is true?
Client:I don't know... maybe 90%.
Therapist:Okay, let's test this together — let's wash one less time today, and see together what happens. Like a scientific experiment.
Client:I'm scared... but okay, let's try.
Key observation: Kənan's ritual gave short-term relief, but strengthened the obsession in the long run — this is precisely the theoretical basis of the ERP model.

🎯 How I apply this in session

I never told Kənan to "just stop washing" — that's unrealistic. Instead, we framed it as an "experiment," and he observed the outcome himself.

Therapeutic Assessment Form

This form carries particular weight in OCD, because the treatment plan is built directly from the map of the obsession-compulsion cycle.

While filling out this table with Kənan, I included the family in the "perpetuating factors" row too — because the reassurance parents gave "just to be sure" was actually reinforcing the ritual, without them realizing it.

CBT assessment tests and scales

These are the tools I use for OCD — both to measure severity and to build the ERP plan.

📊

Y-BOCS (Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale)

The gold-standard clinical tool for assessing the severity of obsessions and compulsions.

📊

CY-BOCS

The child- and adolescent-adapted version of the Y-BOCS.

📊

OCI-R (Obsessive Compulsive Inventory-Revised)

A brief self-report tool covering six symptom dimensions.

📊

Padua Inventory

For a broad assessment of distressing thoughts and neutralizing behaviors.

📊

FOCI (Florida Obsessive Compulsive Inventory)

A brief, practical tool for rapid screening, usable in the very first session.

Note: Kənan's CY-BOCS score dropped from the "moderate-severe" range to "mild" over 10 weeks — objective proof that ERP was working.
🏠

Sample homework

Here's the specific assignment I gave Kənan.

Once a day, touch a door handle and wait five minutes instead of immediately washing your hands. During these five minutes, note your anxiety level (0-10) every minute. You'll see that the anxiety comes down on its own after a while — even if you do nothing. This shows your brain the message: "I can tolerate this anxiety, the ritual isn't required."

Warning signs not to miss during ERP

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

In the Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) model, the line between a worsening of OCD symptoms and a genuine crisis isn't always clear to trainees — the following define that line:

  • Harm obsessions, especially around harming someone else, showing signs of turning into genuine intent;
  • Time spent on rituals reaching several hours a day, with functioning completely paralyzed;
  • Expressions of "because I'm a bad person," accompanied by deep shame and concealment, combined with hopelessness;
  • Family members' accommodation of the rituals increasingly breaking down family functioning.
  1. Ask a direct, non-judgmental question to distinguish whether harm obsessions have turned into intent — in OCD this is almost always ego-dystonic (the client is afraid of it, doesn't want it).
  2. If the degree of functional paralysis is high (3+ hours of ritual a day), offer a psychiatric consultation alongside ERP.
  3. If family accommodation is identified, build a concrete step-down plan in a parent session.
  4. If hopelessness and shame deepen, add a mood screening (PHQ-9) — OCD-depression comorbidity is high.

Note: ERP's very nature is to deliberately raise anxiety — this isn't the protocol failing, it's a sign it's working correctly; the real risk comes from confusing clinical worsening with therapeutic tension.

📌 Module summary

  • OCD is a cycle of obsession, anxiety, and compulsion — the compulsion doesn't solve the problem, it reinforces it.
  • ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) is the gold-standard treatment.
  • "Thought-action fusion" — equating a thought with an action — is the core distortion to question.
  • A family's reassurance-giving behavior can unconsciously reinforce the compulsion.
  • A relapse-prevention plan is an integral part of the final session.
