Module 15 · Health Psychology

A Program for Working with Chronic Illness

In roughly half of people living with chronic pain, additional anxiety or a drop in mood is observed — the body and mind don't work separately from each other. Below is a CBT-based program for psychological support with clients living with chronic illness. This program doesn't treat the illness — it makes living with it easier.

1

Practical technique: Redirecting Pain Attention

When I started working with Fərid, his first question was — "will the pain go away?" I gave an honest answer: maybe not, maybe a little. But how much space the pain takes up in his life — that we could change.

The technique is simple, but it takes practice. When pain intensifies, we redirect attention not entirely to the pain, but to five parts of the surrounding environment (sound, color, temperature) — the pain doesn't disappear, but the mind stops holding it as the sole focus point. After a while, clients learn to make this shift automatically on their own. The first attempts often feel unsuccessful to the client — that's normal, this skill, like any skill, strengthens with repetition.

Attention: This technique doesn't mean denying the pain. On the contrary, it's the choice to accept the pain without giving it all of your attention.
2

Clinical definition: The Biopsychosocial Model

Clinical definition: Per Engel's biopsychosocial model, living with chronic illness is under the mutual influence of biological (pain, symptoms), psychological (mood, cognitive interpretations), and social (support, role changes) factors. In this context, CBT targets pain perception, illness-related catastrophizing, and coping skills — it changes not the illness itself, but the psychological way of "coping" with it.
In plain terms: Living with a chronic illness isn't just physical, it's also a very large emotional burden. Stress, anxiety, and sadness can intensify pain perception — this doesn't mean the pain is "imaginary," it's just that the body and mind are tightly connected.

Fərid:I'm no longer the me I used to be. The illness runs everything.
Therapist:The illness has become a part of your life, that's true. But your whole self?
Fərid:I don't know... sometimes it feels like the whole thing.
Therapist:Let's search together — who are you beyond the illness?
Note: In chronic illness, the question of identity is very often a more difficult topic to work through than the pain itself.
5

Energy Management: Pacing

There's a really common cycle in chronic illness — on a "good day," people try to do everything at once, then spend several days in bed with severe exhaustion. We call this cycle "boom-bust" in clinical language. Pacing breaks this cycle — distributing energy evenly through the day, keeping the boundary even on a "good day."

It seems easy to apply, but it's psychologically hard — because the client resists limiting themselves "when they feel good." I explain it this way: this isn't reducing your strength, it's spreading your strength over a longer time.

Fərid's experience: He used to work in the garden for hours on a good day, then stay in bed for three days. Now he works 20 minutes every day, neither more nor less — the result became more stable. In the first weeks it was really hard for him to hold himself back, then once he felt the difference, he kept going on his own.

Homework

This week's practice: Have the client deliberately cut their activity in half on a "good" day and note how they feel the next day. Seeing the difference with their own eyes is more convincing than words. After a few weeks of doing this, these notes themselves become the best evidence.

Fərid's story: getting acquainted with himself, not his illness

A therapist working with chronic illness has a first duty — not to treat the illness (that's the doctor's job), but to prevent the illness from becoming the person's identity.

📋 Presentation

Fərid was 34 years old, diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis 2 years earlier. Though he kept working, he felt he was "no longer the me I used to be," and increasingly avoided meeting friends. His doctor had recommended psychological support, because his pain scale didn't match his medical indicators — stress was intensifying the pain.

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed that Fərid's main difficulty wasn't the pain itself, it was the thinking built around a "sick identity" — the belief "I'm no longer good for anything" was causing social isolation, and isolation increased stress, which intensified the pain. A classic chronic illness cycle: pain → isolation → stress → more pain.

Therapist:Fərid, when you say "I'm not the me I used to be," what exactly do you mean?
Client:Before I was active, I played football with my friends. Now even if there's no pain on a given day, I can't go — I'm afraid I'll go, and then it'll get worse.
Therapist:So fear is holding you back from movement, and lack of movement...
Client:Isolates me more, I know that. But I don't know how to find the balance.
Therapist:That's exactly why we'll learn the pacing technique — instead of "all or nothing," returning to activity in small doses matched to your energy.
Core principle: In chronic illness, the "sick identity" itself can become a source of stress. The goal of therapy isn't to deny the illness, it's to reframe it as just one part of the identity.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Fərid I ran pacing and cognitive restructuring in parallel — working with the thought alone wasn't enough, the behavior also had to gradually change.

Assessment scales

📊

IPQ-R (Illness Perception Questionnaire)

A tool mapping how the client perceives the illness (beliefs about cause, control, outcome).

📊

PHQ-15

A brief tool measuring the severity of somatic symptoms, useful for tracking the stress-pain connection.

📊

HADS (Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale)

A tool specially designed to measure anxiety and depression in clients with a somatic illness, without confusing them with somatic symptoms.

Note: Fərid's IPQ-R result showed he held the belief "I have no control" about the illness — this became exactly the main target of the cognitive work, because restoring a sense of control plays a direct role in pain management.
12

The medical-psychological boundary in somatic symptoms

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

It's important that in the somatic work component, a psychological explanation doesn't replace medical assessment — the following situations require mandatory medical consultation:

  • The emergence of a new symptom that wasn't there before (e.g. a sudden headache, vision disturbance);
  • A symptom's character being one-sided, progressive, or accompanied by night pain;
  • "Red flag" signs such as weight loss, fever being added to the somatic complaints;
  • A strong connection between the symptom and "secondary gain" in the family (avoiding school) — even though this indicates a psychological component, it doesn't cancel out the need for medical exclusion.
  1. Before the first session, or when a new symptom arises over time, check whether a pediatric assessment has been done.
  2. If red flag signs are detected, pause the psychological work and make an urgent medical referral.
  3. After medical exclusion is confirmed, discuss the symptom's functional role (secondary gain) with the family sensitively.
  4. Firmly avoid framing the symptom as "imaginary" — this damages the therapeutic relationship and leads to concealment.

Note: A somatic symptom is a completely real physical experience for the child, even if it has a psychological origin — explaining this distinction to the parent is the key to the program's success.

"The illness is a part of life, not the whole of it." — A health psychology principle

📌 Module summary

  • The body and mind are tightly connected — stress can intensify pain perception, that doesn't mean the pain is "imaginary."
  • Pacing — distributing energy evenly — breaks the "good day/bad day" cycle.
  • Preserving a sense of identity beyond the illness is important for long-term adjustment.
  • The attention redirection technique doesn't deny the pain, it just prevents it from taking up all the attention.
  • Multidisciplinary support — psychological, occupational therapy, social — completes the full picture.
