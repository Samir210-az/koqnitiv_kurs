Module 16 · Mood Disorders

Depression — A 15-Session Program

Have you ever had a client tell you "I don't have the strength to get out of bed," while by every physical measure they're perfectly healthy? In depression, that's one of the sentences I hear most. What follows is a full program built on Behavioral Activation and cognitive restructuring. It looks simple on paper, but run without rushing it, it produces remarkably strong results.

1

Understanding the cycle

When I started working with Vusal, he kept saying "I don't have the strength to go to work." But looking closer, we found that the real issue wasn't strength at all — it was a cycle. Low mood → passivity → even lower mood. The less you do, the worse you feel, and the worse you feel, the less you do. When I draw this cycle out for a client, it's often the first time they see their own condition as something that actually makes sense.

2

Scientific definition: Major Depressive Disorder

Clinical definition: Per the DSM-5-TR, a major depressive episode is a clinical condition lasting at least two weeks, marked by depressed mood or loss of interest/pleasure, accompanied by changes in sleep, appetite, energy, concentration, and sense of self-worth. Behavioral Activation is one of the CBT components with the strongest empirical support for breaking the passivity/low-mood cycle.
In plain terms: Depression often creates an "energy-drain cycle": you feel bad → you don't want to do anything → inactivity makes you feel even worse. Breaking that cycle — moving in small steps even when your mood isn't ready for it — turns out to be remarkably effective.
3

Practical technique: Behavioral Activation

The clinical principle is clear — motivation isn't waited for, it's created through action. The thought "I'll do it once my mood improves" works backwards in depression, because mood doesn't improve without action first.

Here's what I worked through with Vusal, step by step. First I had him log his actual activities across a week — when he slept, when he sat. Then, next to each activity, he rated his mood 0-10. The result spoke for itself — hours in front of the TV correlated with low mood, while even a short walk nudged the mood rating up. He saw this in his own numbers, not in my words. That's exactly where this method gets its power — I'm not "proving" anything to him, he's discovering it himself.

Note: The activities you choose shouldn't be "big." Large goals like "I'll go to the gym tomorrow" carry a high risk of failure. "I'll step onto the balcony for 5 minutes today" is far more realistic, and far more likely to succeed.
4

Sample dialogue (with Vusal)

Therapist:Let's look at this week's log. Which day was your mood relatively better?
Vusal:Wednesday. I chatted with my neighbor for a bit that day.
Therapist:Interesting. What do you make of that?
Vusal:Maybe... talking helps me?
Therapist:That's a very valuable observation. Let's try to build a bit more of that into this week.
Note: I never start with "go talk to people" as advice. A conclusion the client draws from their own data lands much harder — it's not my statement, it's their discovery.

How it looks across age groups

👶 Children

  • Indifference toward play and interests
  • Physical complaints (stomach aches, headaches)

🧑 Teenagers

  • Irritability, which often doesn't look like "sadness" at all
  • Increased or decreased social media use

🧑‍🦱 Adults

  • Decline in work functioning, persistent fatigue
  • Self-criticism, a sense of worthlessness

7

Homework

This week's exercise: Have the client pick three activities each day and write down a mood rating (0-10) next to each. No big goals — small, real steps, like opening a window, a short walk, or texting a friend. These logs will be reviewed together at the next session.
8

Vusal's story: one step off the couch

This is the hardest paradox in depression: the thing a person needs most — movement, connection, daily rhythm — is demanded exactly when their energy is at its lowest. Vusal's story shows how we worked through that paradox.

📋 Presentation

Vusal was 41, and had lost his job five months earlier. The first few weeks he searched for work; then gradually he stopped getting off the couch, stopped answering friends' calls, and kept saying "what's the point." His wife was worried he had "shut himself off completely."

🔍 What we found

The assessment showed a classic depressive cycle: passivity → no sense of accomplishment → the thought "I'm good for nothing" → even more passivity. Vusal had also personalized the job loss as a personal failure, even though the actual cause was the company's downsizing program.

Therapist:Vusal, what would happen if you got off the couch tomorrow morning and went for a 10-minute walk?
Client:It won't help. This is just how I am now, a walk isn't going to change that.
Therapist:I understand — that thought feels very logical right now. But could we try it anyway — not waiting for a result, just as an experiment?
Client:What do you mean, an experiment?
Therapist:We're not waiting for motivation — because in depression, motivation usually shows up after action, not before it. We take a small action, then we look at the result.
Client:(At the next session) I went out. Nothing dramatic changed, but... I did feel a bit lighter.
Core principle: In Behavioral Activation, we don't wait for motivation — the small action comes first, and the shift in mood usually follows it.

🎯 How I apply this in session

With Vusal, I deliberately picked a very small first task — a 10-minute walk. A big task raises the risk of failure; a small success is what starts breaking the behavioral cycle.

Assessment scales

📊

PHQ-9

The most widely used tool for a quick assessment of depressive symptom severity, aligned with DSM-5-TR criteria.

📊

BDI-II (Beck Depression Inventory)

A broad measure of cognitive, affective, and somatic symptoms — one of the most widely used scales in clinical research.

📊

HDRS (Hamilton Depression Rating Scale)

A clinician-administered structured tool frequently used to track treatment effectiveness.

Note: Vusal's PHQ-9 score started in the moderate-to-severe range; by week 6 of Behavioral Activation it had dropped into the mild range — a good illustration of the long-term impact of small, consistent steps.
12

Assessing suicide risk in the depression program

⚠️ When urgent intervention is needed

In the depression module, risk assessment isn't an add-on — it's the core of the program. Item 9 of the PHQ-9 needs to be tracked in a structured way at every session:

  • Any positive response on PHQ-9 item 9 (other than "not at all");
  • Language describing a specific plan, method, or timeframe;
  • A history of prior suicide attempts — the strongest prognostic risk factor;
  • A sharp deepening of hopelessness, especially combined with the complete loss of previously active interests;
  • "Farewell"-type behaviors — giving away belongings, unusual messages on social media.
  1. Briefly check PHQ-9 item 9 at the start of every session; on a positive response, move immediately to the Columbia Scale.
  2. If a plan+method+timeframe triad is uncovered, pause the session and arrange urgent psychiatric referral together with the family — never leave the client alone.
  3. Talk concretely with the family about restricting access to medication and sharp objects at home (means restriction).
  4. Even at low risk levels, build a written safety plan together (who to call, where to go).
  5. Document every assessment with a date and outcome — this is the foundation for ongoing monitoring.

Note: In a depression program, an apparent "improvement" can sometimes, paradoxically, coincide with rising risk (the return of energy, a sense of "relief" once a decision is made) — the therapist should stay especially alert to this nuance.
